# Washington

## Key Differences from Other States
- **Community property state**
- **Has state estate tax** (exemption ~$2.193M, increasing to $3M July 2025)
- No state inheritance tax
- **No state income tax**
- **Highest estate tax rates in nation** (10-20%)
- No holographic wills
- QFOBI deduction for family businesses

## Community Property Rules
**Revised Code of Washington Title 26**

### Classification
- All property acquired during marriage is **community property**
- Separate property: before marriage, gifts, inheritance
- Income from separate property is **separate property** (American rule)
- Full step-up in basis at first spouse's death

### Estate Planning Implications
- Each spouse owns 50% of community property
- Full step-up in basis for **both halves** at first death
- Each spouse can dispose of their 50% by will
- Significant income tax savings from double step-up

### Community Property Agreements
- Spouses can agree on property characterization
- Must be in writing and signed by both spouses
- Can convert separate to community (and vice versa)
- Common planning tool in Washington

## Will Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Revised Code of Washington Chapter 11.12

### Execution Requirements (RCW 11.12.020)
- Testator must be **18+ years old**
- Must be of **sound mind**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by testator** (or by another at direction in presence)
- Must be signed by at least **2 competent witnesses**
- Witnesses must sign in testator's presence

### Self-Proving Affidavit (RCW 11.20.020)
- Available and recommended
- Testator and witnesses sign affidavit before notary
- Creates presumption of proper execution
- Avoids need to locate witnesses at probate

### Holographic Wills
- **NOT recognized** in Washington
- All wills must be formally witnessed
- Out-of-state holographic wills may be valid if valid where executed

### Witness Requirements
- Must be competent witnesses
- Must sign in testator's presence
- Should not be beneficiaries (best practice)
- Interested witness does not automatically invalidate

## Trust Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: RCW Title 11, Chapter 11.98 (Washington Trust Act)

### Trust Creation (RCW 11.98.005)
- Washington Trust Act governs
- Must have trust property
- Must have ascertainable beneficiaries (or valid purpose)
- Settlor must have capacity
- Writing required for real property

### Trust Administration
- Washington Trust Act governs
- Prudent Investor Rule applies
- Trustee has duties of loyalty, impartiality
- Accounting to beneficiaries required
- Notice upon settlor's death

### Revocable Trusts (RCW 11.103)
- Common for probate avoidance
- Settlor retains control during lifetime
- Presumed revocable unless stated otherwise
- Becomes irrevocable at settlor's death

### Directed Trusts
- Washington allows directed trusts
- Trust advisor can direct trustee
- Trustee liability limited when following directions

### Trust Decanting (RCW 11.107)
- Authorized under Washington law
- Allows modification of irrevocable trusts
- Trustee discretionary power required

## Power of Attorney
**Statutory Basis**: RCW Chapter 11.125 (Uniform Power of Attorney Act)

### Execution Requirements (RCW 11.125.050)
- Must be **signed by principal** (or at direction)
- Must be **notarized** (acknowledged)
- Witnesses not required but recommended
- Statutory form available

### Durability (RCW 11.125.040)
- Durable by default unless document states otherwise
- Non-durable POA terminates upon incapacity
- Springing POA available

### Statutory Form (RCW 11.125.400)
- Washington provides statutory form (recommended)
- Other forms valid if properly executed
- Third parties must accept valid POA

### Agent Duties
- Fiduciary duty to principal
- Must act in principal's best interest
- Must keep records
- Must avoid conflicts of interest

### Hot Powers (RCW 11.125.240)
- Certain powers require express grant:
  - Create, amend, revoke trusts
  - Make gifts
  - Create/change beneficiary designations
  - Create/change rights of survivorship

### Third Party Acceptance (RCW 11.125.340)
- Third parties must accept valid POA
- May request certification
- Refusal may result in liability

## Healthcare Directive
**Statutory Basis**: RCW Chapter 70.122 (Natural Death Act)

### Types Available
- Health Care Directive (combined form)
- Durable Power of Attorney for Health Care
- Living Will (Directive to Physicians)

### Execution Requirements (RCW 70.122.030)
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by declarant** (or at direction)
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults**
- Witnesses cannot be:
  - Attending physician
  - Related to declarant
  - Beneficiaries under will

### Agent Authority
- Make healthcare decisions when principal incapacitated
- Access medical records
- Consent to or refuse treatment
- Arrange care and placement
- Make anatomical gift decisions

### Washington POLST
- Available in Washington
- Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment
- Medical orders for seriously ill patients
- Completed with healthcare provider
- Portable across healthcare settings
- Bright green form

### Default Surrogate (RCW 7.70.065)
- If no healthcare directive:
  - Guardian
  - Durable POA for healthcare
  - Spouse or state registered domestic partner
  - Adult child
  - Parent
  - Adult sibling

## Estate Tax

### Washington Estate Tax
- **State estate tax applies**
- **Exemption**: $2,193,000 (2024, indexed)
- **Increased to $3,000,000** effective July 1, 2025
- **No portability** of exemption between spouses
- **Top rate**: 20% (**HIGHEST in nation**)

### Rate Structure (RCW 83.100.040)
| Taxable Estate | Rate |
|----------------|------|
| $0 - $1M | 10% |
| $1M - $2M | 14% |
| $2M - $3M | 15% |
| $3M - $4M | 16% |
| $4M - $6M | 18% |
| $6M - $7M | 19% |
| $7M - $9M | 19.5% |
| Over $9M | 20% |

### Highest Rates in Nation
- 20% top rate is highest state estate tax rate
- Creates urgency for aggressive planning
- Consider domicile change for very large estates

### QFOBI Deduction (RCW 83.100.046)
- **Qualified Family-Owned Business Interest** deduction
- Can reduce taxable estate for family businesses
- Up to $2.5 million deduction
- Must meet qualifying requirements
- Valuable for business succession planning

### Filing Requirements
- Return due 9 months after death
- Extension available
- File with Washington Department of Revenue
- Must file if gross estate exceeds exemption

### No Inheritance Tax
- **No state inheritance tax**
- **No state gift tax**
- **No state income tax**
- Federal estate tax applies if over federal threshold
- 2025 federal exemption: $13.99 million

### Planning Implications
- **Highest rates require aggressive planning**
- Credit shelter trusts critical for married couples
- ILIT essential for life insurance
- QFOBI deduction for business owners
- Lifetime gifting to reduce estate
- Consider domicile change for very large estates
- No income tax partially offsets estate tax burden

## Medicaid Planning (Apple Health)

### Look-Back Period
- **60 months** (5 years)

### Asset Limits (2025)
- Individual: $2,000
- Married (community spouse): CSRA up to federal maximum (~$154,140)
- CSRA minimum: approximately $31,584

### Income Limits
- Approximately $2,829/month for individuals
- Qualified Income Trust (Miller Trust) available if over limit

### Exempt Assets
- Primary residence (equity limit ~$713,000)
- One vehicle
- Personal belongings and household goods
- Prepaid irrevocable burial contracts
- Term life insurance
- Whole life with face value under $1,500

### Washington-Specific Programs
- **Apple Health** is Washington's Medicaid program
- **Community First Choice** (K waiver)
- **COPES** waiver for home-based services
- Estate recovery program active

### Spousal Protections
- Community spouse resource allowance (CSRA)
- Minimum monthly maintenance needs allowance (MMMNA)
- Court order for increased CSRA available
- Community property affects planning

### Penalty Divisor
- Approximately $10,000-12,000/month (verify current rate - higher cost state)

## Homestead Protection
**RCW 6.13**

### Exemption Amount
- Up to **$125,000** in equity protected
- Applies to dwelling and land

### Requirements
- Must be **principal residence**
- Must be owner
- Declaration recorded (recommended)
- Automatic protection in most cases

### Scope of Protection
- Protected from most judgment creditors
- Does NOT protect against:
  - Mortgages and deeds of trust
  - Property tax liens
  - Mechanic's liens
  - Child support/alimony

### Limited Protection
- $125,000 is moderate given Seattle/metro real estate values
- Consider additional asset protection strategies
- Trust planning for larger equity

## Transfer on Death Deed
**RCW 64.80**

### Features
- Transfer real property on death **without probate**
- Revocable during owner's lifetime
- Does not affect owner's rights during lifetime

### Requirements
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed** by owner
- Must be **notarized** (acknowledged)
- Must be **recorded before death** with county auditor
- Must designate beneficiary

### Advantages
- Avoids probate for real property
- Simple and cost-effective
- Step-up in basis at death
- Revocable during lifetime

### Limitations
- Subject to decedent's debts
- Subject to claims of creditors
- Only for Washington real property

### Revocation
- Record revocation instrument
- Subsequent TOD deed supersedes prior
- Conveyance during life revokes
- Not revoked by will

## Probate Procedures

### Nonintervention Administration (RCW 11.68)
- Personal representative acts without court supervision
- Must be authorized in will or by heirs
- Preferred method in Washington
- Similar to Texas independent administration

### Intervention Administration (RCW 11.76)
- Full court supervision
- Required if not authorized for nonintervention
- More expensive and time-consuming

### Small Estate Procedures (RCW 11.62)
- **Affidavit procedure** for estates under **$100,000** in personal property
- 40-day waiting period after death
- No real property included

### Timeline
- Nonintervention: 6-12 months typical
- Intervention: 12-24 months
- Small estate: 2-4 months
- Creditor claim period: 4 months from notice

### Court Jurisdiction
- **Superior Court** handles probate
- County where decedent resided has jurisdiction

## Safe Deposit Box Rules

### Access After Death
- Joint renter has immediate access
- Without joint renter: letters testamentary from superior court required
- Washington allows examination with death certificate to locate will
- Personal representative needed for full access

### Joint Ownership
- Surviving joint renter retains full access
- No automatic freeze upon death
- Contents accessible without court involvement
- Community property rules apply to contents

### Community Property Note
- Washington is community property state
- Box contents characterized as community or separate property
- Important for estate and estate tax planning

### Estate Tax Considerations
- Washington has estate tax ($2.193M threshold, up to 20% rate)
- Contents valued for estate tax - document carefully
- High rates require accurate valuation

### Best Practices
- Original will should NOT be stored in safe deposit box
- Document contents for estate tax reporting
- Document community vs. separate property in box
- Inform personal representative of box location

## State Charitable Incentives

### Limited State Credits
- Washington has no state income tax
- No state-level charitable deductions or credits
- Federal charitable planning applies

### Estate Planning Implications
- Federal charitable deductions available
- Charitable bequests reduce taxable estate (critical at high rates)
- ILIT removes life insurance from estate
- Charitable lead trusts for high estate tax planning
- Consider charitable remainder trusts
