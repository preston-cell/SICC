# Oklahoma

## Key Differences from Other States
- No state estate tax
- No state inheritance tax
- Common law property state
- **Unlimited homestead value** (up to 160 acres rural)
- Transfer on Death Deed available
- Holographic wills recognized
- Oil/gas mineral rights planning significant

## Will Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Oklahoma Statutes Title 84

### Execution Requirements (84 O.S. § 55)
- Testator must be **18+ years old**
- Must be of **sound mind**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by testator** (or by another at direction in presence)
- Must be signed by at least **2 competent witnesses**
- Witnesses must sign in testator's presence

### Self-Proving Affidavit (84 O.S. § 55)
- Available and recommended
- Testator and witnesses sign affidavit before notary
- Creates presumption of proper execution
- Avoids need to locate witnesses at probate

### Holographic Wills (84 O.S. § 54)
- **Recognized** if entirely in testator's handwriting and signed
- No witness requirement
- Date recommended but not required

### Witness Requirements
- Must be competent witnesses
- Interested witness does not invalidate will
- Should not be beneficiaries (best practice)

## Trust Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: 60 O.S. § 175.1 et seq. (Oklahoma Trust Act)

### Trust Creation (60 O.S. § 175.6)
- Oklahoma Trust Act governs
- Must have trust property
- Must have ascertainable beneficiaries (or valid purpose)
- Settlor must have capacity
- Writing required for real property

### Trust Administration
- Oklahoma Trust Act governs
- Prudent Investor Rule applies
- Trustee has duties of loyalty, impartiality
- Accounting to beneficiaries required
- Notice upon settlor's death

### Revocable Trusts (60 O.S. § 175.41)
- Common for probate avoidance
- Settlor retains control during lifetime
- Presumed revocable unless stated otherwise
- Becomes irrevocable at settlor's death

### Directed Trusts
- Oklahoma allows directed trusts
- Trust advisor can direct trustee
- Trustee liability limited when following directions

### Trust Decanting
- Available under Oklahoma law
- Allows modification of irrevocable trusts
- Trustee discretionary power required

## Power of Attorney
**Statutory Basis**: 15 O.S. § 1001 et seq. (Uniform Power of Attorney Act)

### Execution Requirements (15 O.S. § 1005)
- Must be **signed by principal** (or at direction)
- Must be **notarized** (acknowledged)
- Witnesses not required but recommended
- Statutory form available

### Durability (15 O.S. § 1004)
- Durable if includes durability language
- Non-durable POA terminates upon incapacity
- Springing POA available

### Statutory Form (15 O.S. § 1060)
- Oklahoma provides statutory form (recommended)
- Other forms valid if properly executed
- Third parties must accept valid POA

### Agent Duties
- Fiduciary duty to principal
- Must act in principal's best interest
- Must keep records
- Must avoid conflicts of interest

### Hot Powers (15 O.S. § 1020)
- Certain powers require express grant:
  - Create, amend, revoke trusts
  - Make gifts
  - Create/change beneficiary designations
  - Create/change rights of survivorship

### Third Party Acceptance (15 O.S. § 1040)
- Third parties must accept valid POA
- May request certification
- Refusal may result in liability

## Healthcare Directive
**Statutory Basis**: 63 O.S. § 3101 et seq. (Oklahoma Advance Directive Act)

### Types Available
- Advance Directive for Health Care (combined form)
- Living Will
- Healthcare Proxy

### Execution Requirements (63 O.S. § 3101.4)
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by declarant** (or at direction)
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults**
- Witnesses cannot be:
  - Attending physician
  - Employee of healthcare facility
  - Related to declarant

### Agent Authority
- Make healthcare decisions when principal incapacitated
- Access medical records
- Consent to or refuse treatment
- Arrange care and placement
- Make anatomical gift decisions

### Oklahoma POLST
- Available in Oklahoma
- Medical orders for seriously ill patients
- Completed with healthcare provider
- Portable across healthcare settings

### Default Surrogate (63 O.S. § 3101.8)
- If no advance directive:
  - Guardian
  - Spouse
  - Adult child (majority decides)
  - Parent
  - Adult sibling
  - Nearest adult relative

## Estate and Inheritance Tax
- **No state estate tax**
- **No state inheritance tax**
- **No state gift tax**
- Only federal estate tax applies if estate exceeds federal threshold
- 2025 federal exemption: $13.99 million per person

### Planning Implications
- No state death taxes simplifies planning
- Focus on federal estate tax for large estates
- Unlimited homestead excellent for asset protection
- Oil/gas interests require special planning

## Medicaid Planning (Oklahoma Medicaid)

### Look-Back Period
- **60 months** (5 years)

### Asset Limits (2025)
- Individual: $2,000
- Married (community spouse): CSRA up to federal maximum (~$154,140)
- CSRA minimum: approximately $31,584

### Income Limits
- Approximately $2,829/month for individuals
- Qualified Income Trust (Miller Trust) available if over limit

### Exempt Assets
- Primary residence (equity limit ~$713,000)
- One vehicle
- Personal belongings and household goods
- Prepaid irrevocable burial contracts
- Term life insurance
- Whole life with face value under $1,500

### Oklahoma-Specific Programs
- **Oklahoma Medicaid** (SoonerCare) administered by OHCA
- **ADvantage Waiver** for home-based services
- Estate recovery program active

### Spousal Protections
- Community spouse resource allowance (CSRA)
- Minimum monthly maintenance needs allowance (MMMNA)
- Court order for increased CSRA available

### Penalty Divisor
- Approximately $5,000-6,000/month (verify current rate - lower cost state)

## Homestead Protection
**Okla. Const. Art. XII, § 1; 31 O.S. § 2**

### Exemption Amount
- **Unlimited value** for homestead
- Up to **1 acre** in urban areas
- Up to **160 acres** in rural areas
- One of strongest protections in nation (similar to Texas/Florida)

### Requirements
- Must be **principal residence**
- Must be owner
- Constitutional protection
- Declaration recommended but not required

### Scope of Protection
- Protected from most judgment creditors
- Does NOT protect against:
  - Mortgages and deeds of trust
  - Property tax liens
  - Mechanic's liens (with limitations)
  - Purchase money obligations

### Agricultural Protection
- 160-acre rural exemption protects family farms
- Unlimited value important for agricultural land

## Transfer on Death Deed
**58 O.S. § 1251 et seq.**

### Features
- Transfer real property on death **without probate**
- Revocable during owner's lifetime
- Does not affect owner's rights during lifetime

### Requirements
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed** by owner
- Must be **notarized** (acknowledged)
- Must be **recorded before death** with county clerk
- Must designate beneficiary

### Advantages
- Avoids probate for real property
- Simple and cost-effective
- Step-up in basis at death
- Revocable during lifetime

### Limitations
- Subject to decedent's debts
- Subject to claims of creditors
- Only for Oklahoma real property

### Revocation
- Record revocation instrument
- Subsequent TOD deed supersedes prior
- Conveyance during life revokes
- Not revoked by will

## Probate Procedures

### Regular Probate (58 O.S. § 1 et seq.)
- District Court handles probate
- Personal representative appointed
- Inventory and accounting required
- Bond typically required

### Summary Administration (58 O.S. § 245)
- For smaller estates
- Simplified procedures
- Less court involvement

### Small Estate Affidavit (58 O.S. § 393)
- **Affidavit procedure** for estates under **$50,000** in personal property
- No real property included
- 10-day waiting period after death
- Court filing not required

### Timeline
- Regular administration: 6-12 months typical
- Summary administration: 3-6 months
- Small estate: 1-3 months
- Creditor claim period: 2 months from notice

### Court Jurisdiction
- **District Court** handles probate
- County where decedent resided has jurisdiction

## Unique State Features

### Unlimited Homestead Protection
- One of strongest in nation
- Unlimited value
- Up to 160 acres rural
- Excellent creditor protection

### Oil and Gas Interests
- Major planning consideration in Oklahoma
- Mineral rights require special attention
- Working interests vs. royalty interests
- Consider mineral trusts

### Agricultural State
- Farm and ranch succession planning important
- 160-acre homestead protects family farms
- Entity structures common

### Planning Considerations
- Unlimited homestead provides excellent protection
- No state death taxes simplifies planning
- Oil/gas interests require specialized planning
- TOD deeds widely used
- Agricultural succession planning important

## Safe Deposit Box Rules

### Access After Death
- Joint renter has immediate access
- Without joint renter: letters testamentary from district court required
- Bank may permit examination with death certificate to locate will
- Personal representative appointment needed for full access

### Joint Ownership
- Surviving joint renter has full access rights
- No automatic freeze upon death
- Contents accessible without court involvement
- Important for oil/gas royalty documents and mineral deeds

### Best Practices
- Avoid storing original will in safe deposit box
- Keep mineral rights documents accessible to heirs
- Document box contents with estate planning documents
- Inform executor of box location and contents

## State Charitable Incentives

### Equal Opportunity Education Scholarship Credit
- 50% credit for contributions to scholarship granting organizations
- Individual: up to $1,000 credit ($2,000 contribution)
- Joint: up to $2,000 credit ($4,000 contribution)
- Corporations: 75% credit, up to $100,000
- Supports private school scholarships

### Volunteer Firefighter Tax Credit
- Credit for donations to volunteer fire departments
- Limited but valuable for rural communities

### Agricultural Donation Credit
- Credit for donations of agricultural products to food banks
- Supports Oklahoma agricultural producers

### Estate Planning Implications
- 50% education scholarship credit provides solid tax benefit
- Unlimited homestead + no death taxes = excellent asset protection
- Mineral rights require special estate planning attention
- Consider charitable land trusts for agricultural properties
- Oil/gas working interests may benefit from charitable planning
- Federal planning remains primary focus for death tax planning
