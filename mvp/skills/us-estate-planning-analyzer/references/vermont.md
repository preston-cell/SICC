# Vermont

## Key Differences from Other States
- **Has state estate tax** (exemption $5M)
- No state inheritance tax
- Common law property state
- **Flat 16% rate** (no graduated brackets)
- Exemption not indexed for inflation
- No holographic wills
- No portability

## Will Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Vermont Statutes Title 14

### Execution Requirements (14 V.S.A. § 5)
- Testator must be **18+ years old**
- Must be of **sound mind**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by testator** (or by mark/another at direction)
- Must be signed by at least **2 witnesses** (3 recommended)
- Witnesses must sign in testator's presence

### Self-Proving Affidavit (14 V.S.A. § 9)
- Available and recommended
- Testator and witnesses sign affidavit before notary
- Creates presumption of proper execution
- Avoids need to locate witnesses at probate

### Holographic Wills
- **NOT recognized** in Vermont
- All wills must be formally witnessed
- Out-of-state holographic wills may be valid if valid where executed

### Witness Requirements
- Minimum 2, but **3 recommended**
- Must be competent witnesses
- Must sign in testator's presence
- Should not be beneficiaries (best practice)
- Interested witness does not automatically invalidate

## Trust Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Vermont Statutes Title 14A (Vermont Trust Code)

### Trust Creation (14A V.S.A. § 402)
- Vermont Trust Code adopted
- Must have trust property
- Must have ascertainable beneficiaries (or valid purpose)
- Settlor must have capacity
- Writing required for real property

### Trust Administration
- Vermont Trust Code governs
- Prudent Investor Rule applies
- Trustee has duties of loyalty, impartiality
- Accounting to beneficiaries required
- Notice upon settlor's death

### Revocable Trusts (14A V.S.A. § 602)
- Common for probate avoidance
- Settlor retains control during lifetime
- Presumed revocable unless stated otherwise
- Becomes irrevocable at settlor's death

### Directed Trusts
- Vermont allows directed trusts
- Trust advisor can direct trustee
- Trustee liability limited when following directions

### Trust Decanting
- Available under Vermont law
- Allows modification of irrevocable trusts
- Trustee discretionary power required

## Power of Attorney
**Statutory Basis**: Vermont Statutes Title 14, Chapter 125

### Execution Requirements (14 V.S.A. § 3503)
- Must be **signed by principal** (or at direction)
- Must be **dated**
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults**
- Must be **notarized** (acknowledged)
- All requirements mandatory

### Durability (14 V.S.A. § 3502)
- Durable if includes durability language
- Non-durable POA terminates upon incapacity
- Springing POA available

### Statutory Form
- Vermont provides statutory form (recommended)
- Other forms valid if properly executed
- Third parties must accept valid POA

### Agent Duties
- Fiduciary duty to principal
- Must act in principal's best interest
- Must keep records
- Must avoid conflicts of interest

### Hot Powers
- Certain powers require express grant:
  - Create, amend, revoke trusts
  - Make gifts
  - Create/change beneficiary designations

### Third Party Acceptance
- Third parties must accept valid POA
- May request certification
- Refusal may result in liability

## Healthcare Directive
**Statutory Basis**: Vermont Statutes Title 18, Chapter 231 (Advance Directive Registry)

### Types Available
- Advance Directive (combined form)
- Durable Power of Attorney for Health Care
- Terminal Care Document

### Execution Requirements (18 V.S.A. § 9703)
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by principal** (or at direction)
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults**
- Witnesses cannot be:
  - Healthcare provider
  - Agent named in directive
  - Related to principal

### Vermont Advance Directive Registry
- Vermont maintains advance directive registry
- Voluntary registration available
- Helps ensure directive is accessible
- Healthcare providers can access

### Agent Authority
- Make healthcare decisions when principal incapacitated
- Access medical records
- Consent to or refuse treatment
- Arrange care and placement
- Make anatomical gift decisions

### Vermont POLST (COLST)
- Clinician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment
- Available in Vermont
- Medical orders for seriously ill patients
- Completed with healthcare provider
- Portable across healthcare settings

### Default Surrogate (18 V.S.A. § 9711)
- If no healthcare directive:
  - Guardian
  - Spouse or civil union partner
  - Adult child
  - Parent
  - Adult sibling
  - Other close friend or relative

## Estate Tax

### Vermont Estate Tax
- **State estate tax applies**
- **Exemption**: $5,000,000 per person
- **Not indexed** for inflation
- **No portability** of exemption between spouses
- **Flat rate**: 16%

### Flat Rate (32 V.S.A. § 7442a)
- **16% flat rate** on taxable estate above exemption
- No graduated brackets
- Simpler calculation than most states
- Higher rate than some graduated systems

### No Cliff Effect
- Only excess over $5M exemption is taxed
- More favorable than MA/RI cliff effect
- Graduated vs. cliff is significant distinction

### Filing Requirements
- Return due 9 months after death
- Extension available
- File with Vermont Department of Taxes
- Must file if gross estate exceeds exemption

### No Inheritance Tax
- **No state inheritance tax**
- **No state gift tax**
- Federal estate tax applies if over federal threshold
- 2025 federal exemption: $13.99 million

### Planning Implications
- Moderate exemption ($5M) means fewer estates affected
- Flat 16% rate can be significant
- Credit shelter trusts important (no portability)
- Lifetime gifting to stay below exemption
- Consider domicile if choosing between VT and no-tax state

## Medicaid Planning (Vermont Medicaid/Green Mountain Care)

### Look-Back Period
- **60 months** (5 years)

### Asset Limits (2025)
- Individual: $2,000
- Married (community spouse): CSRA up to federal maximum (~$154,140)
- CSRA minimum: approximately $31,584

### Income Limits
- Approximately $2,829/month for individuals
- Qualified Income Trust (Miller Trust) available if over limit

### Exempt Assets
- Primary residence (equity limit ~$713,000)
- One vehicle
- Personal belongings and household goods
- Prepaid irrevocable burial contracts
- Term life insurance
- Whole life with face value under $1,500

### Vermont-Specific Programs
- **Vermont Medicaid** (Green Mountain Care)
- **Choices for Care** waiver for home-based services
- **Dr. Dynasaur** for children (not estate planning relevant)
- Estate recovery program active

### Spousal Protections
- Community spouse resource allowance (CSRA)
- Minimum monthly maintenance needs allowance (MMMNA)
- Court order for increased CSRA available

### Penalty Divisor
- Approximately $10,000-11,000/month (verify current rate)

## Homestead Protection
**27 V.S.A. § 101**

### Exemption Amount
- Up to **$125,000** in equity protected
- Applies to dwelling and land

### Requirements
- Must be **principal residence**
- Must be owner
- Automatic protection (no filing required)
- Declaration can strengthen protection

### Scope of Protection
- Protected from most judgment creditors
- Does NOT protect against:
  - Mortgages and deeds of trust
  - Property tax liens
  - Mechanic's liens
  - Child support/alimony

### Moderate Protection
- $125,000 is moderate for New England
- Consider supplemental asset protection strategies

## Transfer on Death Deed
- **Not available** in Vermont
- Use revocable trusts for probate avoidance
- Joint ownership with right of survivorship available
- Consider trust ownership for real property

## Probate Procedures

### Regular Probate (14 V.S.A. Chapter 41)
- Probate court handles estates
- Executor/administrator appointed
- Inventory and accounting required
- Bond typically required

### Small Estate Procedures
- Summary administration for smaller estates
- Affidavit procedure available for very small estates

### Timeline
- Regular administration: 6-12 months typical
- Small estate: 2-4 months
- Complex estates: 12-24 months
- Creditor claim period: 4 months from notice

### Court Jurisdiction
- **Probate Court** handles estates
- Each county has probate court
- County where decedent resided has jurisdiction

## Unique State Features

### Flat 16% Rate
- Simpler than graduated rates
- Can be higher than some graduated systems
- No calculations needed for rate
- Predictable tax amount

### No Cliff Effect
- Unlike MA/RI, only excess over exemption taxed
- More favorable for estates near threshold
- Gradual rather than sudden tax

### $5M Exemption
- Moderate exemption
- Fewer estates affected than low-threshold states
- Not indexed (may become less valuable over time)

### Rural State
- Agricultural planning important
- Second home planning for out-of-state owners
- Land conservation planning common
- Farm succession considerations

### Planning Considerations
- Flat 16% rate requires planning
- Credit shelter trusts important (no portability)
- Lifetime gifting to reduce estate
- Consider domicile for larger estates
- Agricultural and conservation planning
- Second home ownership considerations

## Safe Deposit Box Rules

### Access After Death
- Joint renter has immediate access
- Without joint renter: letters testamentary from probate court required
- Bank may permit examination with death certificate to locate will
- Executor needed for full access

### Joint Ownership
- Surviving joint renter retains full access
- No automatic freeze upon death
- Contents accessible without probate involvement

### Estate Tax Considerations
- Vermont has estate tax ($5M threshold, 16% flat rate)
- Contents valued for estate tax purposes
- Document valuable items

### Best Practices
- Original will should NOT be stored in safe deposit box
- Document contents for estate tax reporting
- Inform executor of box location
