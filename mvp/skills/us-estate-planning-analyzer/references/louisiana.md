# Louisiana

## Key Differences from Other States
- **Community property state** (based on civil law/Napoleonic Code)
- No state estate tax
- No state inheritance tax
- **Forced heirship** provisions (unique in US)
- **Civil law system** (different from all other states)
- Successions instead of probate
- Usufruct rights for surviving spouse
- Parish system instead of counties

## Community Property Rules

### Civil Law Foundation
- Louisiana's system based on French/Spanish civil law
- Terminology and concepts differ from other CP states
- "Acquets and gains" instead of community property
- Louisiana Civil Code governs (not statutes like other states)

### Classification (La. C.C. Art. 2338)
- All property acquired during marriage is community property
- Separate property includes: property owned before marriage, gifts, inheritance
- Income from separate property may remain separate (varies by source)
- Fruits (income) of separate property: complex rules apply

### Transmutation
- Written declaration can change property character
- Must be signed by both spouses
- Clear intent required

### Estate Planning Implications
- Each spouse owns 50% of community property
- **Full step-up in basis** for both halves at first spouse's death
- **Forced heirship restricts testamentary freedom**
- Usufruct provides surviving spouse protection

## Forced Heirship
**Louisiana Civil Code Articles 1493-1514**

### Forced Heirs (La. C.C. Art. 1493)
- Children under **24 years old**
- Children of **any age** who are permanently incapable of caring for themselves due to mental incapacity or physical infirmity
- These heirs have **forced portion** (legitime) rights

### Forced Portion (Legitime) (La. C.C. Art. 1495)
- If **one forced heir**: 1/4 of estate
- If **two or more forced heirs**: 1/2 of estate
- **Cannot disinherit forced heirs** (limited exceptions for just cause)

### Disposable Portion
- Portion of estate that can be freely disposed
- 3/4 if one forced heir
- 1/2 if two or more forced heirs
- 100% if no forced heirs

### Disinherison (La. C.C. Art. 1621)
- Limited grounds for disinheriting forced heirs:
  - Child strikes parent
  - Child guilty of cruelty or injury to parent
  - Child attempts to take parent's life
  - Child accuses parent of capital crime (falsely)
  - Child fails to communicate for 2+ years without just cause

### Estate Planning Implications
- **Must account for forced heirship in all estate plans**
- Limited ability to disinherit minor children
- Can disinherit adult children (over 24) who aren't incapacitated
- Strategic planning needed for blended families
- Out-of-state wills may not comply with Louisiana requirements

## Will Requirements
**Louisiana Civil Code Articles 1570-1595**

### Types of Wills

**1. Notarial Will (La. C.C. Art. 1577)** - Most Common
- Must be signed by testator
- Signed before **notary** and **2 witnesses**
- Notary reads will aloud OR testator declares contents
- Most secure form
- Immediately effective without need to locate witnesses

**2. Olographic (Holographic) Will (La. C.C. Art. 1575)**
- **Entirely** in testator's handwriting
- **Dated** (day, month, year required)
- **Signed** by testator
- No witness requirement
- Probate requires proof of handwriting

**3. Statutory Will (La. C.C. Art. 1578)**
- Simplified form available
- Must be signed before notary and 2 witnesses
- Testator need not be able to read

### Execution Requirements (Notarial Will)
- Testator must be **16+ years old**
- Must be of **sound mind**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be signed by testator, notary, and **2 competent witnesses**
- Notary must be present throughout execution
- Declaration of intent required

### Foreign Wills
- Out-of-state wills may be valid if:
  - Valid where executed, OR
  - Valid under Louisiana law
- Must still comply with forced heirship rules

## Trust Requirements
**Louisiana Trust Code (La. R.S. 9:1721 et seq.)**

### Trust Creation (La. R.S. 9:1752)
- Must be **in writing**
- More formal requirements than most states
- Trust income taxed at entity level in Louisiana
- Must have trust property and beneficiaries

### Trust Administration
- Louisiana Trust Code governs
- Prudent Investor Rule applies
- Trustee duties specified by statute
- Accounting to beneficiaries required

### Revocable Trusts
- Available for probate avoidance
- Settlor retains control during lifetime
- Becomes irrevocable at settlor's death
- Subject to forced heirship rules

### Inter Vivos vs. Testamentary
- Inter vivos trusts created during lifetime
- Testamentary trusts created by will
- Both subject to Louisiana formalities

## Power of Attorney (Mandate/Procuration)
**Louisiana Civil Code Articles 2985-3034**

### Terminology
- Called "mandate" or "procuration" under civil law
- Agent called "mandatary"
- Principal called "mandator"

### Execution Requirements
- Must be **signed by principal**
- **Authentic form** (notarized) recommended
- Can be in private form with witnesses
- Notarization required for real estate transactions

### Types
- **General mandate**: broad powers
- **Special mandate**: limited specific powers
- **Durable mandate**: survives incapacity

### Durability (La. R.S. 9:3851 et seq.)
- Durable if includes durability language
- Non-durable terminates upon incapacity
- Springing mandate available

### Agent Powers
- Must be specifically enumerated
- Certain powers require express grant:
  - Make gifts
  - Create/modify trusts
  - Real estate transactions
  - Change beneficiary designations

## Healthcare Directive
**La. R.S. 40:1151 et seq.**

### Types Available
- Living Declaration (Living Will)
- Healthcare Power of Attorney
- Combined Advance Directive

### Living Declaration Requirements (La. R.S. 40:1151.2)
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by declarant** (or at direction)
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults**
- Notarization recommended but not required
- Witnesses cannot be:
  - Related by blood or marriage
  - Entitled to any portion of estate

### Healthcare Proxy
- Must be signed and witnessed
- Agent must be adult
- Cannot be healthcare provider unless related

### Agent Authority
- Make healthcare decisions when principal incapacitated
- Access medical records
- Consent to or refuse treatment
- Arrange care and placement

### LaPOST (Louisiana Physician Orders for Scope of Treatment)
- Available for seriously ill patients
- Medical orders completed with healthcare provider
- Portable across healthcare settings
- Yellow form

## Estate and Inheritance Tax
- **No state estate tax**
- **No state inheritance tax**
- **No state gift tax**
- Only federal estate tax applies if estate exceeds federal threshold
- 2025 federal exemption: $13.99 million per person

## Medicaid Planning (Louisiana Medicaid)

### Look-Back Period
- **60 months** (5 years)

### Asset Limits (2025)
- Individual: $2,000
- Married (community spouse): CSRA up to federal maximum (~$154,140)
- CSRA minimum: approximately $31,584

### Income Limits
- Approximately $2,829/month for individuals
- Qualified Income Trust (Miller Trust) available if over limit

### Exempt Assets
- Primary residence (equity limit ~$713,000)
- One vehicle
- Personal belongings and household goods
- Prepaid irrevocable burial contracts
- Term life insurance
- Whole life with face value under $1,500

### Louisiana-Specific Programs
- **Louisiana Medicaid** administered by Louisiana Department of Health
- **Community Choices Waiver** for home-based services
- **PACE** programs in some areas
- Estate recovery program active

### Community Property and Medicaid
- Community property rules affect eligibility
- CSRA protects community spouse's share
- Usufruct may affect asset treatment
- Both spouses' assets considered available

### Penalty Divisor
- Approximately $6,500-7,500/month (verify current rate)

## Homestead Protection
**Louisiana Constitution Article XII, Section 9; La. R.S. 20:1**

### Exemption Amount
- Up to **$35,000** of equity
- Up to **160 acres** in rural areas (agricultural lands)
- Up to **5 acres** in urban areas
- Limited compared to some states

### Requirements
- Must be **principal residence**
- Must be owner or head of family
- Filing declaration recommended

### Scope of Protection
- Protected from most judgment creditors
- Does NOT protect against:
  - Mortgages and liens on the property
  - Property tax liens
  - Federal tax liens
  - Debts for improvements to the homestead

## Succession Process (Not "Probate")
**Louisiana Code of Civil Procedure**

### Terminology
- "Succession" not "probate"
- "Decedent" not "testator" (for intestate)
- "Legatee" not "beneficiary"
- "Executor" or "Succession Representative" administers estate
- "Judgment of Possession" transfers property

### Types of Succession

**Independent Administration (La. C.C.P. Art. 3396)**
- If will provides, minimal court oversight
- Executor acts without court approval
- Most efficient method

**Simple Putting in Possession (La. C.C.P. Art. 3001)**
- For small estates
- Simplified procedures
- No formal administration required

**Judicial Partition (La. C.C.P. Art. 1300)**
- When disputes exist
- Court divides property
- More expensive and time-consuming

### Small Succession (La. C.C.P. Art. 3421)
- Gross value under **$125,000**
- Simplified affidavit procedure
- No formal administration required

### Timeline
- Simple succession: 3-6 months
- Complex succession: 12-24 months
- Contested succession: 2+ years

### Court Jurisdiction
- **District Court** handles successions
- Parish where decedent was domiciled has jurisdiction

## Usufruct (Life Estate Equivalent)
**Louisiana Civil Code Articles 535-629**

### Legal Usufruct (La. C.C. Art. 890)
- Surviving spouse has usufruct over deceased spouse's community property
- If children from the marriage exist
- Terminates upon remarriage or death
- **Cannot sell naked ownership without consent**

### Testamentary Usufruct
- Can grant usufruct in will
- More flexible than legal usufruct
- Can extend beyond legal usufruct period
- Common planning tool for surviving spouse

### Naked Ownership
- Children receive "naked ownership" subject to usufruct
- Cannot force sale of property
- Receive full ownership when usufruct terminates

## Unique State Features

### Civil Law System
- **Different legal concepts** than all other states
- Requires Louisiana-licensed attorney
- Out-of-state documents often don't work
- Civil Code governs instead of common law

### Forced Heirship
- **Major planning constraint** unique to Louisiana
- Cannot freely disinherit minor children
- Plan around forced portion limitations
- Consider life insurance outside estate

### Parish System
- Louisiana has parishes, not counties
- Succession filed in parish of domicile
- 64 parishes in Louisiana

### Immovable vs. Movable Property
- "Immovable" = real property
- "Movable" = personal property
- Different rules for each type
- Different transfer requirements

### Planning Considerations
- **Use Louisiana-specific documents** (out-of-state forms often invalid)
- Account for forced heirship in all planning
- Consider usufruct for surviving spouse
- Community property provides basis step-up
- Succession process differs from probate
- Trust planning must comply with Louisiana Trust Code
- Blended families require careful forced heirship analysis
- Life insurance can bypass forced heirship rules

## Safe Deposit Box Rules

### Access After Death
- Joint renter has immediate access
- Without joint renter: court order from district court required
- Louisiana's civil law system has unique procedures
- Succession representative appointment needed

### Joint Ownership
- Surviving joint renter has access rights
- No automatic freeze upon death
- Community property rules may affect contents ownership

### Community Property Note
- Box contents characterized as community or separate property
- Forced heirship rules may apply to valuable contents
- Proper characterization critical

### Best Practices
- Original will should NOT be stored in safe deposit box
- Community property characterization important
- Forced heirship considerations for valuable items
- Inform succession representative of box location
