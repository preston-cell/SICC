# Montana

## Key Differences from Other States
- No state estate tax
- No state inheritance tax
- Common law property state
- **Strong homestead protection** ($350,000)
- Transfer on Death Deed available
- **40% Qualified Endowment Tax Credit** (one of highest in nation)
- Holographic wills recognized
- Uniform Probate Code adopted
- Agricultural/ranch estate planning significant

## Will Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Montana Code Annotated Title 72 (Uniform Probate Code)

### Execution Requirements (Mont. Code Ann. § 72-2-522)
- Testator must be **18+ years old**
- Must be of **sound mind**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by testator** (or by another at direction in presence)
- Must be signed by at least **2 witnesses**
- Witnesses must sign within reasonable time after witnessing

### Self-Proving Affidavit (Mont. Code Ann. § 72-2-524)
- Available and recommended
- Testator and witnesses sign affidavit before notary
- Creates presumption of proper execution
- Avoids need to locate witnesses at probate

### Holographic Wills (Mont. Code Ann. § 72-2-522)
- **Recognized** if material provisions in testator's handwriting and signed
- No witness requirement
- Date recommended but not required
- Practical for remote/rural situations

### Witness Requirements
- Must be competent witnesses
- Interested witness does not invalidate will
- Should not be beneficiaries (best practice)

## Trust Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Mont. Code Ann. Title 72, Chapter 38 (Montana Uniform Trust Code)

### Trust Creation (Mont. Code Ann. § 72-38-402)
- Montana Uniform Trust Code adopted
- Must have trust property
- Must have ascertainable beneficiaries (or valid purpose)
- Settlor must have capacity
- Writing required for real property

### Trust Administration
- Montana UTC governs
- Prudent Investor Rule applies
- Trustee has duties of loyalty, impartiality
- Accounting to beneficiaries required
- Notice upon settlor's death

### Revocable Trusts (Mont. Code Ann. § 72-38-602)
- Common for probate avoidance
- Settlor retains control during lifetime
- Presumed revocable unless stated otherwise
- Becomes irrevocable at settlor's death

### Directed Trusts (Mont. Code Ann. § 72-38-808)
- Montana allows directed trusts
- Trust advisor/protector can direct trustee
- Trustee liability limited when following directions

### Trust Decanting
- Available under Montana law
- Allows modification of irrevocable trusts
- Trustee discretionary power required

## Power of Attorney
**Statutory Basis**: Mont. Code Ann. Title 72, Chapter 31 (Uniform Power of Attorney Act)

### Execution Requirements (Mont. Code Ann. § 72-31-205)
- Must be **signed by principal** (or at direction)
- Must be **notarized** (acknowledged)
- Witnesses not required but recommended
- Statutory form available

### Durability (Mont. Code Ann. § 72-31-204)
- Durable by default unless document states otherwise
- Non-durable POA terminates upon incapacity
- Springing POA available

### Statutory Form (Mont. Code Ann. § 72-31-360)
- Montana provides statutory form (recommended)
- Other forms valid if properly executed
- Third parties must accept valid POA

### Agent Duties
- Fiduciary duty to principal
- Must act in principal's best interest
- Must keep records
- Must avoid conflicts of interest

### Hot Powers (Mont. Code Ann. § 72-31-320)
- Certain powers require express grant:
  - Create, amend, revoke trusts
  - Make gifts
  - Create/change beneficiary designations
  - Create/change rights of survivorship

### Third Party Acceptance (Mont. Code Ann. § 72-31-240)
- Third parties must accept valid POA
- May request certification
- Refusal may result in liability

## Healthcare Directive
**Statutory Basis**: Mont. Code Ann. Title 50, Chapter 9

### Types Available
- Declaration of Desire for Natural Death (Living Will) (§ 50-9-103)
- Appointment of Health Care Agent (§ 50-9-106)
- Combined Advance Directive

### Execution Requirements
**Living Will (Mont. Code Ann. § 50-9-103)**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by declarant**
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults**
- Notarization alternative available

**Health Care Agent (Mont. Code Ann. § 50-9-106)**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by principal**
- Must be **notarized** OR **witnessed by 2 adults**

### Agent Authority
- Make healthcare decisions when principal incapacitated
- Access medical records
- Consent to or refuse treatment
- Arrange care and placement
- Make anatomical gift decisions

### Montana POLST
- Available in Montana
- Medical orders for seriously ill patients
- Completed with healthcare provider
- Portable across healthcare settings

### Default Surrogate (Mont. Code Ann. § 50-9-106)
- If no healthcare directive:
  - Guardian
  - Spouse
  - Adult child
  - Parent
  - Adult sibling
  - Close friend

## Estate and Inheritance Tax
- **No state estate tax**
- **No state inheritance tax**
- **No state gift tax**
- Only federal estate tax applies if estate exceeds federal threshold
- 2025 federal exemption: $13.99 million per person

### Planning Implications
- No state death taxes simplifies planning
- Focus on federal estate tax for large estates
- Strong charitable incentives (40% credit)
- Probate avoidance for privacy and efficiency

## Medicaid Planning (Montana Medicaid)

### Look-Back Period
- **60 months** (5 years)

### Asset Limits (2025)
- Individual: $2,000
- Married (community spouse): CSRA up to federal maximum (~$154,140)
- CSRA minimum: approximately $31,584

### Income Limits
- Approximately $2,829/month for individuals
- Qualified Income Trust (Miller Trust) available if over limit

### Exempt Assets
- Primary residence (equity limit ~$713,000)
- One vehicle
- Personal belongings and household goods
- Prepaid irrevocable burial contracts
- Term life insurance
- Whole life with face value under $1,500

### Montana-Specific Programs
- **Montana Medicaid** administered by DPHHS
- **Big Sky Waiver** for home and community based services
- Estate recovery program active
- Special considerations for agricultural assets

### Spousal Protections
- Community spouse resource allowance (CSRA)
- Minimum monthly maintenance needs allowance (MMMNA)
- Court order for increased CSRA available

### Penalty Divisor
- Approximately $8,000-9,000/month (verify current rate)

## Homestead Protection
**Mont. Code Ann. § 70-32-104**

### Exemption Amount
- Up to **$350,000** in equity protected
- Strong protection compared to many states
- Applies to dwelling and land

### Requirements
- Must be **primary residence**
- Must be owner
- Declaration recommended but automatic protection exists
- Only one homestead per family

### Scope of Protection
- Protected from most judgment creditors
- Does NOT protect against:
  - Mortgages and deeds of trust
  - Property tax liens
  - Mechanic's liens
  - Child support/alimony

### Agricultural Considerations
- Protects farm/ranch home
- May include land reasonably necessary for use
- Significant for agricultural families

## Transfer on Death Deed
**Mont. Code Ann. § 72-6-401 et seq.**

### Features
- Transfer real property on death **without probate**
- Revocable during owner's lifetime
- Does not affect owner's rights during lifetime
- Practical for Montana real property

### Requirements
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed** by owner
- Must be **notarized** (acknowledged)
- Must be **recorded before death** with county clerk and recorder
- Must designate beneficiary

### Advantages
- Avoids probate for real property
- Simple and cost-effective
- Step-up in basis at death
- Revocable during lifetime

### Limitations
- Subject to decedent's debts
- Medicaid estate recovery applies
- Subject to claims of creditors
- Only for Montana real property

### Revocation
- Record revocation instrument
- Subsequent TOD deed supersedes prior
- Conveyance during life revokes
- Not revoked by will

## Probate Procedures

### Informal Probate (Mont. Code Ann. § 72-3-303)
- Available for uncontested estates
- Minimal court involvement
- Personal representative appointed by clerk
- Most common form

### Formal Probate (Mont. Code Ann. § 72-3-401)
- Required if will contested
- Full court supervision
- More expensive and time-consuming

### Small Estate Procedures (Mont. Code Ann. § 72-3-1101)
- **Affidavit procedure** for estates under **$50,000** in personal property
- 30-day waiting period after death
- No real property included (or use TOD deed)
- Court filing not required

### Summary Administration
- For small estates with real property
- Simplified court process
- Faster distribution

### Timeline
- Informal probate: 6-12 months typical
- Formal probate: 12-24 months
- Small estate: 2-4 months
- Creditor claim period: 4 months from notice

### Court Jurisdiction
- **District Court** handles probate
- County where decedent resided has jurisdiction

## Safe Deposit Box Rules

### Access After Death
- Joint renter has immediate access
- Without joint renter: letters testamentary required
- Bank may permit examination with death certificate to locate will
- District court appoints personal representative

### Joint Ownership
- Surviving joint renter retains full access
- No automatic freeze on death
- Contents accessible without court involvement
- Important for agricultural operations with time-sensitive documents

### Best Practices
- Avoid storing original will in safe deposit box
- Keep ranch/farm documents accessible to heirs
- Document box contents
- Inform personal representative of location

## State Charitable Incentives

### Qualified Endowment Tax Credit
- **40% credit** for contributions to qualified Montana endowments
- Maximum credit: $10,000 individual / $20,000 joint
- **One of the highest charitable credit rates in the nation**
- Applies to permanent endowments at Montana nonprofits

### Infrastructure Tax Credit
- Credit for contributions to infrastructure projects
- Supports rural community development

### College Savings Plan Deduction
- Deduction for contributions to Montana 529 plans
- Up to $3,000 per beneficiary per year

### Estate Planning Implications
- 40% endowment credit is exceptionally valuable
- Among highest state charitable credit rates available
- Coordinate large charitable gifts with Montana residency
- Can combine state credit with federal deduction
- Consider establishing Montana endowment fund during lifetime
- Strong homestead + no death taxes = favorable estate planning environment
