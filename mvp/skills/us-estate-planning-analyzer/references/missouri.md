# Missouri

## Key Differences from Other States
- No state estate tax
- No state inheritance tax
- Common law property state
- **Beneficiary Deed** widely available and used
- Higher Medicaid asset limit ($5,000)
- Limited homestead protection ($15,000)
- No holographic wills
- Multiple 50% charitable tax credits

## Will Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Missouri Revised Statutes Chapter 474

### Execution Requirements (Mo. Rev. Stat. § 474.320)
- Testator must be **18+ years old**
- Must be of **sound mind**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by testator** (or by another at direction in presence)
- Must be signed by at least **2 witnesses**
- Witnesses must sign in presence of testator

### Self-Proving Affidavit (Mo. Rev. Stat. § 474.337)
- Available and recommended
- Testator and witnesses sign affidavit before notary
- Creates presumption of proper execution
- Avoids need to locate witnesses at probate

### Holographic Wills
- **NOT recognized** in Missouri
- All wills must be formally witnessed
- Out-of-state holographic wills may be valid if valid where executed

### Witness Requirements
- Must be competent witnesses
- Must sign in presence of testator
- Should not be beneficiaries (best practice)
- Interested witness does not automatically invalidate

## Trust Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Mo. Rev. Stat. Chapter 456 (Missouri Uniform Trust Code)

### Trust Creation (Mo. Rev. Stat. § 456.4-402)
- Missouri Uniform Trust Code adopted
- Must have trust property
- Must have ascertainable beneficiaries (or valid purpose)
- Settlor must have capacity
- Writing required for real property

### Trust Administration
- Missouri UTC governs
- Prudent Investor Rule applies
- Trustee has duties of loyalty, impartiality
- Accounting to beneficiaries required
- Notice upon settlor's death

### Revocable Trusts (Mo. Rev. Stat. § 456.6-602)
- Common for probate avoidance
- Settlor retains control during lifetime
- Presumed revocable unless stated otherwise
- Becomes irrevocable at settlor's death

### Directed Trusts (Mo. Rev. Stat. § 456.8-808)
- Missouri allows directed trusts
- Trust advisor/protector can direct trustee
- Trustee liability limited when following directions

### Trust Decanting (Mo. Rev. Stat. § 456.4-419)
- Authorized under Missouri law
- Allows modification of irrevocable trusts
- Trustee discretionary power required
- Notice requirements apply

## Power of Attorney
**Statutory Basis**: Mo. Rev. Stat. Chapter 404

### Execution Requirements (Mo. Rev. Stat. § 404.705)
- Must be **signed by principal** (or at direction)
- Must be **notarized** (acknowledged)
- Witnesses not required but recommended
- Statutory form available

### Durability (Mo. Rev. Stat. § 404.705)
- Durable if includes durability language
- Non-durable POA terminates upon incapacity
- Springing POA available

### Statutory Form (Mo. Rev. Stat. § 404.723)
- Missouri provides statutory form (recommended)
- Other forms valid if properly executed
- Third parties must accept valid POA

### Agent Duties
- Fiduciary duty to principal
- Must act in principal's best interest
- Must keep records
- Must avoid conflicts of interest

### Powers Granted
- General powers under § 404.710
- Specific enumerated powers available
- Real estate powers require specific grant
- Gift-making powers require specific grant

### Third Party Acceptance (Mo. Rev. Stat. § 404.717)
- Third parties must accept valid POA
- May request certification
- Reasonable refusal permitted
- Bad faith refusal may result in liability

## Healthcare Directive
**Statutory Basis**: Mo. Rev. Stat. Chapter 459

### Types Available
- Durable Power of Attorney for Health Care (§ 404.800)
- Declaration (Living Will) (§ 459.015)
- Combined Advance Directive

### Execution Requirements
**Durable Power of Attorney for Health Care (§ 404.822)**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by principal**
- Must be **notarized**
- Witnesses not required but recommended

**Living Will Declaration (§ 459.015)**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by declarant**
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults**

### Agent Authority
- Make healthcare decisions when principal incapacitated
- Access medical records
- Consent to or refuse treatment
- Arrange care and placement
- Make anatomical gift decisions

### Missouri MOST
- Medical Orders for Scope of Treatment
- Available for seriously ill patients
- Completed with healthcare provider
- Portable across healthcare settings
- Yellow form

### Default Surrogate
- Missouri has limited statutory default surrogate rules
- Healthcare POA strongly recommended
- Guardian may be needed without directive

## Estate and Inheritance Tax
- **No state estate tax**
- **No state inheritance tax**
- **No state gift tax**
- Only federal estate tax applies if estate exceeds federal threshold
- 2025 federal exemption: $13.99 million per person

### Planning Implications
- No state death taxes simplifies planning
- Focus on federal estate tax for large estates
- Probate avoidance still valuable
- Beneficiary deed very useful for real property

## Medicaid Planning (MO HealthNet)

### Look-Back Period
- **60 months** (5 years)

### Asset Limits (2025)
- Individual: **$5,000** (higher than most states)
- Married (community spouse): CSRA up to federal maximum (~$154,140)
- CSRA minimum: approximately $31,584

### Income Limits
- Approximately $2,829/month for individuals
- Qualified Income Trust (Miller Trust) available if over limit

### Exempt Assets
- Primary residence (equity limit ~$713,000)
- One vehicle
- Personal belongings and household goods
- Prepaid irrevocable burial contracts
- Term life insurance
- Whole life with face value under $1,500

### Missouri-Specific Programs
- **MO HealthNet** is Missouri's Medicaid program
- **MO HealthNet for Aged, Blind and Disabled**
- Home and Community Based Services waivers
- Estate recovery program active

### Spousal Protections
- Community spouse resource allowance (CSRA)
- Minimum monthly maintenance needs allowance (MMMNA)
- Court order for increased CSRA available

### Penalty Divisor
- Approximately $6,000-7,000/month (verify current rate)

## Homestead Protection
**Mo. Rev. Stat. § 513.475**

### Exemption Amount
- Up to **$15,000** in equity protected
- Applies to dwelling and land
- Limited compared to other states

### Requirements
- Must be **principal residence**
- Must be owner (or head of family)
- Automatic protection (no filing required)
- Only one homestead per family

### Scope of Protection
- Protected from most judgment creditors
- Does NOT protect against:
  - Mortgages and deeds of trust
  - Property tax liens
  - Mechanic's liens
  - Child support/alimony

### Alternative Protections
- Tenancy by entirety for married couples
- Trust ownership
- Retirement account protections
- Life insurance cash value protections

## Beneficiary Deed
**Mo. Rev. Stat. § 461.001 et seq.**

### Features
- Transfer real property on death **without probate**
- Revocable during owner's lifetime
- Does not affect owner's rights during lifetime
- Simple and cost-effective

### Requirements
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed** by owner
- Must be **notarized** (acknowledged)
- Must be **recorded before death** with county recorder
- Must designate grantee-beneficiary

### Advantages
- Avoids probate for real property
- Maintains complete control during life
- No gift tax consequences during life
- Step-up in basis at death
- Can be revoked or changed easily

### Limitations
- Subject to decedent's debts
- Medicaid estate recovery applies
- Subject to claims of creditors
- Only for Missouri real property

### Revocation
- Record revocation instrument
- Subsequent beneficiary deed supersedes prior
- Conveyance during life revokes
- Not revoked by will

## Probate Procedures

### Informal Administration (Mo. Rev. Stat. § 473.090)
- Available for estates over small estate threshold
- Less court supervision
- Personal representative has broader authority
- Most common form

### Formal Administration
- Full court supervision
- Required for contested estates
- More expensive and time-consuming

### Small Estate Procedures (Mo. Rev. Stat. § 473.097)
- **Affidavit procedure** for estates under **$40,000** in personal property
- 30-day waiting period after death
- **Refusal of letters** available for small estates
- Summary distribution possible

### Timeline
- Regular administration: 6-12 months typical
- Small estate: 2-4 months
- Complex estates: 12-24 months
- Creditor claim period: 6 months from first publication

### Court Jurisdiction
- **Probate Division of Circuit Court** handles estates
- County where decedent resided has jurisdiction

## Unique State Features

### Beneficiary Deed
- Widely used and well-established in Missouri
- Excellent probate avoidance tool
- Simpler than trust for single property
- Very popular for residential real estate

### Higher Medicaid Asset Limit
- $5,000 individual limit more generous
- One of highest in nation
- Provides additional planning flexibility

### Limited Homestead
- Only $15,000 protection
- Significantly lower than many states
- Consider alternative asset protection strategies
- Tenancy by entirety more valuable

### Multiple Charitable Credits
- Several 50% tax credit programs
- Requires pre-approval for most
- Valuable for charitable planning

### Planning Considerations
- Beneficiary deeds widely used and recommended
- Limited homestead requires alternative protection strategies
- Higher Medicaid asset limit advantageous
- No state death taxes simplifies planning
- Charitable credits valuable for giving

## Safe Deposit Box Rules

### Access After Death
- Joint renter has immediate access
- Without joint renter: letters testamentary from probate court required
- Bank may permit limited examination with death certificate
- Probate court can authorize access to search for will

### Joint Ownership
- Surviving joint renter has full access rights
- No automatic freeze on death
- Contents accessible without probate involvement
- Coordinates well with beneficiary deed planning

### Best Practices
- Avoid storing original will in safe deposit box
- Keep beneficiary deed copies accessible
- Document box contents with estate plan
- Inform personal representative of box location

## State Charitable Incentives

### Neighborhood Preservation Act Credit
- 50% state tax credit for donations to qualified neighborhood organizations
- Maximum credit: $100,000 per individual
- Supports community development in targeted areas
- Credit must be pre-approved

### Youth Opportunities and Violence Prevention Credit
- 50% credit for contributions to qualified programs
- Maximum credit varies by program
- Supports at-risk youth programs

### Maternity Home Tax Credit
- 50% credit for donations to maternity homes
- Supports crisis pregnancy services

### Domestic Violence Shelter Credit
- 50% credit for donations to approved shelters

### Estate Planning Implications
- Multiple 50% credit programs offer significant tax efficiency
- Pre-approval required for most credits - plan ahead
- Coordinate lifetime giving with Missouri residency
- No state death taxes means federal planning primary focus
- Community development credits support local giving objectives
