# Wisconsin

## Key Differences from Other States
- **Community property state** (Marital Property Act)
- No state estate tax
- No state inheritance tax
- **Only midwestern community property state**
- Transfer on Death Deed available
- Called "marital property" rather than community property
- No holographic wills

## Community Property Rules (Marital Property)
**Wisconsin Marital Property Act (Wis. Stat. Chapter 766)**

### Wisconsin Marital Property Act (1986)
- Called **"marital property"** rather than community property
- Based on Uniform Marital Property Act (UMPA)
- All property acquired during marriage is marital property
- Provides community property tax benefits

### Classification
- **Marital property**: acquired during marriage
- **Individual property**: before marriage, gifts, inheritance
- Appreciation of individual property generally remains individual
- Income from individual property is **marital property**

### Step-Up in Basis
- Full step-up in basis at first spouse's death
- Applies to **both halves** of marital property
- Significant income tax savings for appreciated assets

### Estate Planning Implications
- Each spouse owns 50% of marital property
- Each spouse can dispose of their 50% by will
- Marital property agreement can modify rules
- Community property benefits for step-up in basis

### Marital Property Agreements (Wis. Stat. § 766.58)
- Can customize property classification
- Convert marital to individual (and vice versa)
- Must be in writing and signed by both spouses
- Can be entered before or during marriage

## Will Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Wisconsin Statutes Chapter 853

### Execution Requirements (Wis. Stat. § 853.03)
- Testator must be **18+ years old**
- Must be of **sound mind**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by testator** (or by mark/another at direction)
- Must be signed by at least **2 competent witnesses**
- Witnesses must sign within reasonable time

### Self-Proving Affidavit (Wis. Stat. § 853.04)
- Available and recommended
- Testator and witnesses sign affidavit before notary
- Creates presumption of proper execution
- Avoids need to locate witnesses at probate

### Holographic Wills
- **NOT recognized** in Wisconsin
- All wills must be formally witnessed
- Out-of-state holographic wills may be valid if valid where executed

### Witness Requirements
- Must be competent witnesses
- Must sign within reasonable time of witnessing
- Should not be beneficiaries (best practice)
- Interested witness does not automatically invalidate

## Trust Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Wisconsin Statutes Chapter 701 (Wisconsin Trust Code)

### Trust Creation (Wis. Stat. § 701.0402)
- Wisconsin Trust Code adopted
- Must have trust property
- Must have ascertainable beneficiaries (or valid purpose)
- Settlor must have capacity
- Writing required for real property

### Trust Administration
- Wisconsin Trust Code governs
- Prudent Investor Rule applies
- Trustee has duties of loyalty, impartiality
- Accounting to beneficiaries required
- Notice upon settlor's death

### Revocable Trusts (Wis. Stat. § 701.0602)
- Common for probate avoidance
- Settlor retains control during lifetime
- Presumed revocable unless stated otherwise
- Becomes irrevocable at settlor's death

### Directed Trusts (Wis. Stat. § 701.0808)
- Wisconsin allows directed trusts
- Trust advisor can direct trustee
- Trustee liability limited when following directions

### Trust Decanting (Wis. Stat. § 701.0418)
- Authorized under Wisconsin law
- Allows modification of irrevocable trusts
- Trustee discretionary power required

## Power of Attorney
**Statutory Basis**: Wisconsin Statutes Chapter 244 (Power of Attorney for Finances)

### Execution Requirements (Wis. Stat. § 244.05)
- Must be **signed by principal** (or at direction)
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults**
- Must be **notarized** (acknowledged)
- All requirements mandatory
- Statutory form available

### Durability (Wis. Stat. § 244.04)
- Durable if includes durability language
- Non-durable POA terminates upon incapacity
- Springing POA available

### Statutory Form (Wis. Stat. § 244.61)
- Wisconsin provides statutory form (recommended)
- Other forms valid if properly executed
- Third parties must accept valid POA

### Agent Duties
- Fiduciary duty to principal
- Must act in principal's best interest
- Must keep records
- Must avoid conflicts of interest

### Hot Powers (Wis. Stat. § 244.41)
- Certain powers require express grant:
  - Create, amend, revoke trusts
  - Make gifts
  - Create/change beneficiary designations
  - Create/change rights of survivorship

### Third Party Acceptance (Wis. Stat. § 244.17)
- Third parties must accept valid POA
- May request certification
- Refusal may result in liability

## Healthcare Directive
**Statutory Basis**: Wisconsin Statutes Chapter 155 (Power of Attorney for Health Care)

### Types Available
- Power of Attorney for Health Care
- Declaration to Physicians (Living Will)
- Combined Advance Directive

### Execution Requirements (Wis. Stat. § 155.10)
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by principal**
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults**
- Witnesses cannot be:
  - Healthcare provider
  - Related to principal
  - Named as agent

### Agent Authority
- Make healthcare decisions when principal incapacitated
- Access medical records
- Consent to or refuse treatment
- Arrange care and placement
- Make anatomical gift decisions

### Wisconsin POLST
- Available in Wisconsin
- Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment
- Medical orders for seriously ill patients
- Completed with healthcare provider
- Portable across healthcare settings

### Default Surrogate (Wis. Stat. § 155.30)
- If no healthcare directive:
  - Guardian
  - Spouse
  - Adult child
  - Parent
  - Adult sibling
  - Any adult relative

## Estate and Inheritance Tax
- **No state estate tax**
- **No state inheritance tax**
- **No state gift tax**
- Only federal estate tax applies if estate exceeds federal threshold
- 2025 federal exemption: $13.99 million per person

### Planning Implications
- No state death taxes simplifies planning
- Focus on federal estate tax for large estates
- Marital property provides automatic step-up
- Probate avoidance for privacy and efficiency

## Medicaid Planning (Wisconsin Medicaid/BadgerCare)

### Look-Back Period
- **60 months** (5 years)

### Asset Limits (2025)
- Individual: $2,000
- Married (community spouse): CSRA up to federal maximum (~$154,140)
- CSRA minimum: approximately $31,584

### Income Limits
- Approximately $2,829/month for individuals
- Qualified Income Trust (Miller Trust) available if over limit

### Exempt Assets
- Primary residence (equity limit ~$713,000)
- One vehicle
- Personal belongings and household goods
- Prepaid irrevocable burial contracts
- Term life insurance
- Whole life with face value under $1,500

### Wisconsin-Specific Programs
- **Wisconsin Medicaid** (BadgerCare Plus)
- **Family Care** managed long-term care
- **IRIS** (Include, Respect, I Self-Direct)
- Estate recovery program active

### Spousal Protections
- Community spouse resource allowance (CSRA)
- Minimum monthly maintenance needs allowance (MMMNA)
- Court order for increased CSRA available
- Marital property affects planning

### Penalty Divisor
- Approximately $8,000-9,000/month (verify current rate)

## Homestead Protection
**Wis. Stat. § 815.20**

### Exemption Amount
- Up to **$75,000** in equity protected
- Applies to dwelling and land

### Requirements
- Must be **principal residence**
- Must be owner
- Automatic protection (no filing required)

### Scope of Protection
- Protected from most judgment creditors
- Does NOT protect against:
  - Mortgages and deeds of trust
  - Property tax liens
  - Mechanic's liens
  - Child support/alimony

### Moderate Protection
- $75,000 is moderate by national standards
- Consider supplemental asset protection strategies

## Transfer on Death Deed
**Wis. Stat. § 705.15**

### Features
- Transfer real property on death **without probate**
- Revocable during owner's lifetime
- Does not affect owner's rights during lifetime

### Requirements
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed** by owner
- Must be **notarized** (acknowledged)
- Must be **recorded before death** with register of deeds
- Must designate beneficiary

### Advantages
- Avoids probate for real property
- Simple and cost-effective
- Step-up in basis at death
- Revocable during lifetime

### Limitations
- Subject to decedent's debts
- Subject to claims of creditors
- Only for Wisconsin real property

### Revocation
- Record revocation instrument
- Subsequent TOD deed supersedes prior
- Conveyance during life revokes
- Not revoked by will

## Probate Procedures

### Informal Administration (Wis. Stat. § 865)
- Available for uncontested estates
- Minimal court involvement
- Personal representative acts independently
- Most common form

### Formal Administration (Wis. Stat. § 856)
- Required if will contested
- Full court supervision
- More expensive and time-consuming

### Summary Assignment (Wis. Stat. § 867.01)
- For estates under **$50,000** in net assets
- Surviving spouse or heirs can petition
- Simplified procedure

### Transfer by Affidavit (Wis. Stat. § 867.03)
- For very small estates
- 30-day waiting period after death
- No court involvement

### Timeline
- Informal administration: 6-12 months typical
- Formal administration: 12-24 months
- Summary: 2-4 months
- Creditor claim period: 3 months from notice

### Court Jurisdiction
- **Circuit Court** handles probate
- County where decedent resided has jurisdiction

## Safe Deposit Box Rules

### Access After Death
- Joint renter has immediate access
- Without joint renter: letters testamentary from circuit court required
- Wisconsin allows examination with death certificate to locate will
- Personal representative needed for full access

### Joint Ownership
- Surviving joint renter retains full access
- No automatic freeze upon death
- Contents accessible without court involvement
- Marital property rules may apply to contents

### Marital Property Note
- Wisconsin is marital property (community property) state
- Box contents characterized as marital or individual property
- Similar to community property in other states
- Document characterization

### Best Practices
- Original will should NOT be stored in safe deposit box
- Document marital vs. individual property in box
- Inform personal representative of box location

## State Charitable Incentives

### Endow Wisconsin Tax Credit
- 25% credit for donations to qualified community foundations
- Up to $250 credit per donor per year
- Must be to permanent endowment fund
- Supports Wisconsin communities

### School Tuition Tax Deduction
- Deduction for private school tuition
- K-12 private school eligible
- Not a credit, but provides tax benefit

### Estate Planning Implications
- Endow Wisconsin credit encourages permanent giving
- Federal charitable deductions available
- Marital property can be used for charitable planning
- Community foundation gifts receive state credit
