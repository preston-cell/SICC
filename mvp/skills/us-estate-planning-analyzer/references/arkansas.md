# Arkansas

## Key Differences from Other States
- No state estate tax
- No state inheritance tax
- Common law property state
- **Dower and curtesy rights still recognized** (unusual - one of few states)
- Homestead protection constitutionally guaranteed
- Simplified small estate procedures
- Holographic wills recognized

## Will Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Arkansas Code Title 28

### Execution Requirements (Ark. Code § 28-25-103)
- Testator must be **18+ years old**
- Must be of **sound mind**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by testator at end of will**
- Must be signed by at least **2 witnesses**
- Witnesses must sign in **presence of testator**
- Witnesses must sign at testator's request

### Self-Proving Affidavit (Ark. Code § 28-25-106)
- Available and recommended
- Signed by testator and witnesses before notary
- Creates presumption of proper execution
- Eliminates need to locate witnesses at probate

### Holographic Wills (Ark. Code § 28-25-104)
- **Recognized** if entirely in testator's handwriting and signed
- **No witness requirement** for holographic wills
- Date recommended but not required
- Material provisions must be in testator's handwriting

### Attestation Clause
- Recommended but not required
- Documents that witnesses saw testator sign
- Helps prove proper execution

### Nuncupative (Oral) Wills
- Recognized in limited circumstances (imminent death)
- Only for personal property under $500
- Requires 2 witnesses
- Generally not recommended

## Trust Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Arkansas Code Title 28, Chapter 73 (Arkansas Trust Code)

### Trust Creation (Ark. Code § 28-73-402)
- No specific statutory formalities beyond common law
- Must have identifiable trust property
- Must have ascertainable beneficiaries
- Settlor must have capacity
- Arkansas Trust Code adopted (based on UTC)

### Trust Administration
- Prudent Investor Rule applies
- Trustee has duty of loyalty, impartiality
- Annual accounting to beneficiaries required
- Court supervision minimal for most trusts

### Revocable Trusts
- Common for probate avoidance
- Settlor retains control during lifetime
- Becomes irrevocable at settlor's death

## Power of Attorney
**Statutory Basis**: Arkansas Code § 28-68-101 et seq. (Uniform Power of Attorney Act)

### Execution Requirements
- Must be **signed by principal**
- Must be **acknowledged (notarized)**
- Durable if includes specific durability language
- Witnesses recommended but not required

### Statutory Form (Ark. Code § 28-68-301)
- Arkansas provides statutory form (recommended)
- Other forms valid if properly executed
- Third parties required to accept valid POA

### Durability (Ark. Code § 28-68-104)
- Must include language: "This power of attorney is not affected by subsequent disability or incapacity of the principal"
- Default is non-durable unless durability language included
- Springing POA available

### Hot Powers (Ark. Code § 28-68-201)
- Certain powers require specific grant:
  - Create, amend, revoke, or terminate trusts
  - Make gifts
  - Create or change beneficiary designations
  - Create or change rights of survivorship
  - Delegate authority

### Termination
- Terminates upon principal's death
- Terminates upon principal's incapacity (unless durable)
- Revocable by competent principal at any time

## Healthcare Directive
**Statutory Basis**: Arkansas Rights of the Terminally Ill or Permanently Unconscious Act (Ark. Code § 20-17-201 et seq.)

### Types Available
- Declaration (Living Will)
- Healthcare Proxy
- Combined Advance Directive

### Execution Requirements (Ark. Code § 20-17-202)
- Must be signed by declarant
- Must be witnessed by **2 adults**
- Witnesses cannot be related by blood or marriage
- Witnesses cannot be entitled to any portion of estate
- Witness cannot be attending physician
- Witness cannot be employee of healthcare facility where declarant is patient

### Living Will Effectiveness
- Operative when:
  1. Terminal condition certified by attending physician
  2. Death imminent regardless of life-sustaining treatment
  3. Declarant unable to make treatment decisions

### Healthcare Proxy Authority
- Make healthcare decisions when principal incapacitated
- Access medical records
- Consent or refuse treatment
- Arrange placement in care facilities

### Revocation
- Can be revoked at any time by declarant
- Revocation effective upon communication to physician
- No specific form required for revocation

## Estate and Inheritance Tax
- **No state estate tax** (Arkansas estate tax repealed)
- **No state inheritance tax**
- **No state gift tax**
- Only federal estate tax applies if estate exceeds federal threshold
- 2025 federal exemption: $13.99 million per person

## Dower and Curtesy Rights
**Unique to Arkansas** (and very few other states)

### Dower (Surviving Wife's Rights) (Ark. Code § 28-11-301)
- Life estate in **1/3 of real property** husband owned during marriage
- **Cannot be defeated by will**
- Attaches to all real property owned during marriage, not just at death
- Must be released during lifetime or barred by valid prenuptial agreement

### Curtesy (Surviving Husband's Rights) (Ark. Code § 28-11-301)
- Life estate in **all real property** wife owned during marriage (if children born)
- Life estate in **1/2** if no children born
- Same release requirements as dower

### Estate Planning Implications
- **Must account for dower/curtesy in all estate plans**
- Spouse must join in deeds to convey clear title
- Can be waived by prenuptial or postnuptial agreement
- Affects trust funding strategies
- Title companies require spouse signature even if property solely owned
- Life estate cannot be devised by will

### Waiver and Release
- Can be released by joining in conveyance
- Can be barred by antenuptial (prenuptial) agreement
- Divorce terminates dower/curtesy rights
- Cannot be unilaterally waived by one spouse

### Impact on Planning
- Revocable trusts must be properly funded with spouse's consent
- Joint tenancy can avoid dower/curtesy issues
- Beneficiary deeds subject to dower/curtesy
- Must address in buy-sell agreements

## Medicaid Planning (Arkansas Medicaid)

### Look-Back Period
- **60 months** (5 years)

### Asset Limits (2025)
- Individual: $2,000
- Married (community spouse): CSRA up to federal maximum (~$154,140)
- CSRA minimum: approximately $31,584

### Income Limits
- Approximately $2,829/month for individuals
- Qualified Income Trust (Miller Trust) available if over limit

### Exempt Assets
- Primary residence (equity limit ~$713,000)
- One vehicle
- Personal belongings and household goods
- Prepaid irrevocable burial contracts
- Term life insurance
- Whole life insurance with face value under $1,500

### Arkansas-Specific Considerations
- **ARChoices** program for home and community-based services
- **PACE** (Program of All-inclusive Care for Elderly) available in some areas
- Lower cost of living affects penalty divisor
- Estate recovery program active

### Penalty Divisor
- Approximately $6,000-7,000/month (verify current rate)

## Homestead Protection
**Constitutional Protection** (Arkansas Constitution Article 9, Section 3-6)

### Exemption Amount
- **Rural**: 160 acres outside city, **unlimited value**
- **Urban**: 1 acre in city, **unlimited value** (or 1/4 acre in city of first class)
- One of strongest homestead protections in nation

### Requirements
- Must be **head of household** or married
- Must be **primary residence**
- Must file homestead declaration for full protection (Ark. Code § 16-66-210)

### Scope of Protection
- Protected from most creditors
- Does NOT protect against:
  - Mortgages and deeds of trust
  - Property tax liens
  - Mechanic's liens
  - Purchase money obligations
- Survives death for benefit of surviving spouse and minor children

### Constitutional Guarantee
- Cannot be waived except by deed or mortgage
- One of few states with constitutional homestead protection
- Very strong creditor protection

## Probate Procedures

### Small Estate Affidavit (Ark. Code § 28-41-101)
- Available for estates under **$100,000**
- Simplified administration for small estates
- 45-day waiting period after death
- No court supervision required for small estates

### Independent Administration
- Available if will authorizes or all heirs agree
- Reduces court involvement and costs
- Personal representative acts without court orders
- Annual accounting still required

### Formal Administration
- Required for larger or contested estates
- Court supervision of all actions
- More expensive and time-consuming

### Timeline
- Small estate: 2-4 months
- Independent administration: 6-12 months
- Formal administration: 12-24 months

### Court Jurisdiction
- **Circuit Court** handles probate in Arkansas
- County where decedent resided has jurisdiction

## Transfer on Death Deed
**Ark. Code § 18-12-608**

### Features
- Available for real property
- Revocable during lifetime
- Avoids probate for real property
- Does not affect ownership during lifetime

### Requirements
- Must be in writing
- Must be signed and acknowledged (notarized)
- Must be recorded before death
- Must identify grantee(s)

### Limitations
- Subject to decedent's debts
- Subject to dower/curtesy rights
- Medicaid estate recovery applies
- Subject to claims of creditors

## Unique State Features

### Small Estate Procedures
- Affidavit procedure for estates under $100,000
- Simplified administration reduces costs
- Faster distribution to beneficiaries

### Independent Administration
- Minimal court supervision when authorized
- Lower legal fees
- Faster estate settlement

### Income Tax Considerations
- No state tax on Social Security benefits
- Favorable retirement income treatment
- No state estate tax burden
- Lower cost of living

### Planning Considerations
- **Always address dower/curtesy rights** in estate planning
- Consider prenuptial agreement to waive dower/curtesy
- Use transfer on death deeds for real property
- Homestead protection provides significant creditor protection
- Coordinate with spouse for all real property transactions
- Joint tenancy avoids dower/curtesy issues

## Safe Deposit Box Rules

### Access After Death
- Joint renter has immediate access
- Without joint renter: letters testamentary from circuit court required
- Bank may allow examination with death certificate to search for will
- Cannot remove contents without proper authority

### Joint Ownership
- Surviving joint renter has full access rights
- No automatic freeze upon death
- Contents accessible without court involvement
- Consider adding fiduciary as authorized signer

### Best Practices
- Original will should NOT be stored in safe deposit box
- Document box contents with estate plan
- Inform executor of box location
- Consider accessibility issues
