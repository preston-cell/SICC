# North Carolina

## Key Differences from Other States
- No state estate tax (repealed 2013)
- No state inheritance tax
- Common law property state
- Elective share protections for spouse
- Holographic wills recognized
- TOD securities but no TOD deed for real property
- Clerk of Superior Court handles probate
- Popular retirement destination

## Will Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: North Carolina General Statutes Chapter 31

### Execution Requirements (N.C.G.S. § 31-3.3)
- Testator must be **18+ years old**
- Must be of **sound mind**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by testator** (or by another at direction in presence)
- Must be signed by at least **2 competent witnesses**
- Testator must sign or acknowledge signature in presence of witnesses
- Witnesses sign in testator's presence

### Self-Proving Affidavit (N.C.G.S. § 31-11.6)
- Available and recommended
- Testator and witnesses sign affidavit before notary
- Creates presumption of proper execution
- Avoids need to locate witnesses at probate

### Holographic Wills (N.C.G.S. § 31-3.4)
- **Recognized** if entirely in testator's handwriting and signed
- Found after testator's death among testator's valuable papers
- OR deposited with someone for safekeeping
- OR in possession of testator at death
- Three witnesses must testify to handwriting

### Witness Requirements
- Must be competent witnesses
- Should not be beneficiaries (best practice)
- Interested witness does not automatically invalidate

## Trust Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: N.C.G.S. Chapter 36C (NC Uniform Trust Code)

### Trust Creation (N.C.G.S. § 36C-4-402)
- North Carolina Uniform Trust Code adopted
- Must have trust property
- Must have ascertainable beneficiaries (or valid purpose)
- Settlor must have capacity
- Writing required for real property

### Trust Administration
- NC UTC governs
- Prudent Investor Rule applies
- Trustee has duties of loyalty, impartiality
- Accounting to beneficiaries required
- Notice upon settlor's death

### Revocable Trusts (N.C.G.S. § 36C-6-602)
- Common for probate avoidance
- Settlor retains control during lifetime
- Presumed revocable unless stated otherwise
- Becomes irrevocable at settlor's death

### Directed Trusts (N.C.G.S. § 36C-8-808)
- North Carolina allows directed trusts
- Trust advisor can direct trustee
- Trustee liability limited when following directions

### Trust Decanting (N.C.G.S. § 36C-8-816.1)
- Authorized under North Carolina law
- Allows modification of irrevocable trusts
- Trustee discretionary power required
- Notice requirements apply

## Power of Attorney
**Statutory Basis**: N.C.G.S. Chapter 32C (Uniform Power of Attorney Act)

### Execution Requirements (N.C.G.S. § 32C-1-105)
- Must be **signed by principal** (or at direction)
- Must be **notarized** (acknowledged)
- Witnesses not required but recommended
- Statutory short form available

### Durability (N.C.G.S. § 32C-1-104)
- Durable if includes durability language
- Non-durable POA terminates upon incapacity
- Springing POA available

### Statutory Short Form (N.C.G.S. § 32C-3-301)
- North Carolina provides statutory short form (recommended)
- Other forms valid if properly executed
- Third parties must accept valid POA

### Agent Duties
- Fiduciary duty to principal
- Must act in principal's best interest
- Must keep records
- Must avoid conflicts of interest

### Hot Powers (N.C.G.S. § 32C-2-201)
- Certain powers require express grant:
  - Create, amend, revoke trusts
  - Make gifts
  - Create/change beneficiary designations
  - Create/change rights of survivorship

### Third Party Acceptance (N.C.G.S. § 32C-1-120)
- Third parties must accept valid POA
- May request certification
- 7 business days to accept or refuse
- Refusal may result in liability

## Healthcare Directive
**Statutory Basis**: N.C.G.S. Chapter 32A (Health Care Power of Attorney and Living Will)

### Types Available
- Health Care Power of Attorney (N.C.G.S. § 32A-15)
- Advance Directive for a Natural Death (Living Will) (N.C.G.S. § 90-321)
- Combined Advance Directive

### Execution Requirements
**Health Care Power of Attorney (N.C.G.S. § 32A-16)**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by principal**
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults** AND **notarized**
- Witnesses cannot be healthcare agent

**Living Will (N.C.G.S. § 90-321)**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by declarant**
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults** AND **notarized**

### Agent Authority
- Make healthcare decisions when principal incapacitated
- Access medical records
- Consent to or refuse treatment
- Arrange care and placement
- Make anatomical gift decisions

### North Carolina MOST
- Medical Orders for Scope of Treatment
- Available for seriously ill patients
- Completed with healthcare provider
- Portable across healthcare settings
- Bright pink form

### Default Surrogate (N.C.G.S. § 90-21.13)
- If no healthcare power of attorney:
  - Guardian
  - Healthcare agent in order designated
  - Spouse
  - Adult child (majority decides if multiple)
  - Parent
  - Adult sibling (majority decides)
  - Adult grandchild (majority decides)

## Estate and Inheritance Tax
- **No state estate tax** (repealed July 1, 2013)
- **No state inheritance tax**
- **No state gift tax**
- Only federal estate tax applies if estate exceeds federal threshold
- 2025 federal exemption: $13.99 million per person

### Planning Implications
- No state death taxes simplifies planning
- Focus on federal estate tax for large estates
- Probate avoidance for privacy and efficiency
- Popular retirement destination due to favorable tax treatment

## Medicaid Planning (NC Medicaid)

### Look-Back Period
- **60 months** (5 years)

### Asset Limits (2025)
- Individual: $2,000
- Married (community spouse): CSRA up to federal maximum (~$154,140)
- CSRA minimum: approximately $31,584

### Income Limits
- Approximately $2,829/month for individuals
- Qualified Income Trust (Miller Trust) available if over limit

### Exempt Assets
- Primary residence (equity limit ~$713,000)
- One vehicle
- Personal belongings and household goods
- Prepaid irrevocable burial contracts
- Term life insurance
- Whole life with face value under $1,500

### North Carolina-Specific Programs
- **NC Medicaid** administered by DHHS
- **CAP/DA** (Community Alternatives Program for Disabled Adults)
- **PACE** programs available
- Estate recovery program active

### Spousal Protections
- Community spouse resource allowance (CSRA)
- Minimum monthly maintenance needs allowance (MMMNA)
- Court order for increased CSRA available

### Penalty Divisor
- Approximately $7,000-8,000/month (verify current rate)

## Homestead Protection
**N.C. Const. Art. X, § 2; N.C.G.S. § 1C-1601**

### Exemption Amount
- Standard: Up to **$35,000** in equity protected
- **Age 65+**: Up to **$60,000** protected
- Applies to dwelling and land
- Additional personal property exemptions

### Requirements
- Must be **principal residence**
- Must be owner
- Automatic protection
- No declaration required

### Scope of Protection
- Protected from most judgment creditors
- Does NOT protect against:
  - Mortgages and deeds of trust
  - Property tax liens
  - Mechanic's liens
  - Child support/alimony

### Enhanced Exemption
- Increased protection for elderly (65+)
- Significant planning opportunity

## Transfer on Death Deed
- **Not authorized** in North Carolina for real property
- TOD registration available for securities
- Use revocable trusts for real property probate avoidance
- Joint ownership with right of survivorship available

## Elective Share
**N.C.G.S. § 30-3.1**

### Features
- Surviving spouse can elect against will
- Claim portion of total estate
- Based on length of marriage
- Cannot be completely disinherited

### Calculation
- Complex formula based on length of marriage
- Ranges from 3% (short marriage) to 50% (15+ year marriage)
- Includes probate and non-probate assets

### Planning Implications
- Affects estate planning for married couples
- Prenuptial agreements may waive
- Must consider elective share in planning

## Probate Procedures

### Regular Administration (N.C.G.S. § 28A)
- **Clerk of Superior Court** handles probate
- Executor/administrator appointed by Clerk
- Inventory required within 3 months
- Accounting required

### Informal Administration
- Available for uncontested estates
- Clerk can grant letters without formal hearing
- Most common form

### Small Estate Procedures (N.C.G.S. § 28A-25-1)
- **Collection by affidavit** for estates under **$20,000** in personal property
- 30-day waiting period after death
- No real property included
- Simplified procedures

### Summary Administration
- For estates under $30,000 to surviving spouse
- Simplified process

### Timeline
- Regular administration: 6-12 months typical
- Small estate: 2-4 months
- Complex estates: 12-24 months
- Creditor claim period: 3 months from publication

### Court Jurisdiction
- **Clerk of Superior Court** handles probate
- County where decedent resided has jurisdiction
- Clerk functions as probate judge

## Safe Deposit Box Rules

### Access After Death
- Joint renter has immediate access
- Without joint renter: letters testamentary from clerk of superior court required
- Bank may allow examination with death certificate to search for will
- NC General Statutes govern access procedures

### Joint Ownership
- Surviving joint renter retains full access
- No automatic freeze upon death
- Contents accessible without estate administration
- Consider for retirees relocating to NC

### Best Practices
- Original will should NOT be stored in safe deposit box
- Many NC clerks recommend keeping original will with attorney
- Document box contents with estate planning documents
- Inform executor of box location

## State Charitable Incentives

### Mill Rehabilitation Tax Credit
- Credit for rehabilitation of historic textile mill properties
- Can apply to charitable donations of preservation easements
- Up to 40% credit for qualified expenditures
- Coordinates with federal historic preservation credits

### Conservation Tax Credit (Modified)
- NC previously had generous conservation credits (repealed 2013)
- Current: limited credit for donated conservation easements
- 25% credit, capped at $250,000 over 5 years

### Education Savings Plan Deduction
- Deduction for NC 529 plan contributions
- Up to $2,500 per beneficiary ($5,000 for joint filers)

### Estate Planning Implications
- Conservation easement credits valuable for land-rich estates
- Coordinate easement donations with estate planning
- No state death taxes simplifies planning
- Popular retirement destination - consider charitable planning before relocation
- Federal charitable strategies remain primary focus
