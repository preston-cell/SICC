# Arizona

## Key Differences from Other States
- **Community property state**
- No state estate tax
- No state inheritance tax
- Strong homestead protection (unlimited value)
- Beneficiary deed available for real property transfer
- Relatively simple probate procedures
- Favorable retirement income tax treatment

## Community Property Rules

### Classification
- All property acquired during marriage is community property
- Separate property includes: property owned before marriage, gifts, inheritance
- Income from separate property is **separate property** (American Rule)
- Commingling can convert separate to community property
- Transmutation requires clear intent and documentation

### Estate Planning Implications
- Each spouse owns 50% of community property
- **Full step-up in basis** for both halves at first spouse's death
- Each spouse can dispose of their 50% by will
- Cannot disinherit spouse's 50% share
- Surviving spouse automatically receives their 50%

### Quasi-Community Property (ARS § 14-2102)
- Property acquired while living outside Arizona treated as community property for estate/divorce purposes
- Applies if it would have been community property had couple lived in Arizona
- Important for couples who moved to Arizona

### Tracing and Characterization
- Burden is on party claiming property is separate
- Clear records essential for separate property claims
- Appreciation of separate property remains separate (if no community effort)

## Will Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Arizona Revised Statutes Title 14 (Uniform Probate Code)

### Execution Requirements (ARS § 14-2502)
- Testator must be **18+ years old**
- Must be of **sound mind**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by testator** (or by someone in testator's presence at direction)
- Must be signed by at least **2 witnesses**
- Witnesses must sign within reasonable time after witnessing signature

### Self-Proving Affidavit (ARS § 14-2504)
- Available and recommended for efficient probate
- Testator and witnesses execute affidavit before notary
- Creates presumption of proper execution
- Avoids need to locate witnesses at probate

### Holographic Wills (ARS § 14-2503)
- **Recognized** if material portions in testator's handwriting and signed
- No witness requirement
- Date recommended but not required

### International Wills
- Arizona recognizes international wills under ARS § 14-2801 et seq.
- Useful for testators with property in multiple countries

## Trust Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: ARS Title 14, Chapter 11 (Arizona Trust Code)

### Trust Creation (ARS § 14-10402)
- No specific statutory formalities beyond common law
- Must be in writing for real property (Statute of Frauds)
- Must have identifiable trust property
- Must have ascertainable beneficiaries

### Community Property Trusts
- Can hold community property and maintain its character
- Important for maintaining step-up in basis benefits
- Must be properly drafted to preserve CP status

### Trust Administration
- Arizona Trust Code governs
- Prudent Investor Rule applies
- Trustee must provide accountings upon request

## Power of Attorney
**Statutory Basis**: ARS § 14-5501 et seq.

### Execution Requirements
- Must be **signed by principal**
- Must be **witnessed by one adult** AND **notarized**
- Durable if includes durability language

### Statutory Form (ARS § 14-5501)
- Arizona provides statutory form (recommended)
- Other forms valid if properly executed
- Third parties must accept valid POA

### Durability (ARS § 14-5501(B))
- Must include language: "This power of attorney is not affected by subsequent disability or incapacity of the principal"
- Without durability language, terminates upon incapacity

### Agent Acceptance
- Agent may accept appointment by exercising power
- Written acceptance recommended

### Hot Powers
- Certain powers require specific grant in POA:
  - Creating or amending trusts
  - Making gifts
  - Creating or changing beneficiary designations
  - Delegating authority

## Healthcare Directive
**Statutory Basis**: ARS § 36-3221 et seq. (Health Care Directives)

### Types Available
- Living Will
- Healthcare Power of Attorney
- Combined Advance Directive
- Mental Health Care Power of Attorney
- Prehospital Medical Care Directive (DNR)

### Execution Requirements (ARS § 36-3221)
- Must be signed by principal
- Must be witnessed by **1 adult** OR **notarized**
- Witness cannot be healthcare agent

### Witness Restrictions
- Witness cannot be designated agent
- Healthcare provider employees may witness (but not recommended)
- Should not be person who will inherit

### Living Will Effectiveness
- Becomes operative when:
  1. Terminal condition exists, AND
  2. Death is imminent regardless of treatment, AND
  3. Patient cannot communicate

### Healthcare Agent Authority
- Make healthcare decisions when principal cannot
- Access medical records
- Consent or refuse treatment
- Arrange care facilities

## Estate and Inheritance Tax
- **No state estate tax** (repealed 2006)
- **No state inheritance tax**
- **No state gift tax**
- Only federal estate tax applies if estate exceeds federal threshold
- 2025 federal exemption: $13.99 million per person

## Medicaid Planning (AHCCCS - Arizona Health Care Cost Containment System)

### Look-Back Period
- **60 months** (5 years)

### Asset Limits (2025)
- Individual: $2,000
- Married (community spouse): CSRA up to federal maximum (~$154,140)
- CSRA minimum: approximately $31,584

### Income Limits
- ALTCS income limit: approximately $2,829/month
- Miller Trust (Qualified Income Trust) available if over income limit

### Exempt Assets
- Primary residence (equity limit ~$713,000)
- One vehicle
- Personal belongings
- Household goods
- Prepaid irrevocable burial contracts
- Term life insurance (no cash value)
- Whole life insurance with face value under $1,500

### Arizona-Specific Considerations
- **AHCCCS** is Arizona's Medicaid with unique managed care structure
- **ALTCS** (Arizona Long Term Care System) for long-term care services
- Community property rules affect asset division for married couples
- Estate recovery program active

### Community Property and Medicaid
- All community property considered available to institutionalized spouse
- CSRA protects portion for community spouse
- Important to properly characterize separate vs. community property
- Spousal refusal available but rarely used

### Penalty Divisor
- Approximately $8,500-9,500/month (verify current rate)

## Homestead Protection
**ARS § 33-1101**

### Exemption Amount
- **Unlimited value exemption** up to $250,000 equity (increased 2022)
- Property cannot exceed 10 acres

### Requirements
- Must be primary residence
- Automatically applies (no filing required)
- Must be head of family or single person

### Scope of Protection
- Protected from most judgment creditors
- Does NOT protect against:
  - Mortgages and deeds of trust
  - Property tax liens
  - Mechanic's liens
  - IRS tax liens
  - Child support/alimony

## Beneficiary Deed
**ARS § 33-405**

### Features
- Transfer real property on death without probate
- Revocable during owner's lifetime
- Does not affect owner's rights during lifetime
- Simple alternative to trust for real property

### Requirements
- Must be in writing
- Must be **recorded before owner's death**
- Must identify beneficiary by name
- Must include specific statutory language
- Must be signed and acknowledged (notarized)

### Limitations
- Subject to debts of estate
- Medicaid can recover against property
- Not effective if beneficiary predeceases owner (unless alternate named)

## Probate Procedures

### Small Estate Affidavit (ARS § 14-3971)
- Personal property under **$75,000**
- 30-day waiting period after death
- No court filing required
- Claimant signs affidavit under penalty of perjury

### Summary Administration (ARS § 14-3973)
- Real property under **$100,000**
- Must petition court
- Simplified process

### Informal Probate
- Available for uncontested estates
- Court involvement minimal
- Personal representative appointed by registrar
- Most common form of probate

### Formal Probate
- Required if will contested
- Full court supervision
- More expensive and time-consuming

### Timeline
- Informal probate: 6-12 months typical
- Formal probate: 1-2 years or longer

## Unique State Features

### Simplified Probate
- Small estate affidavit for personal property under $75,000
- Summary administration for real property under $100,000
- Informal probate available for uncontested estates
- Relatively efficient compared to many states

### Spousal Protection
- Surviving spouse has right to remain in homestead
- Allowance for family support during administration
- Community property provides automatic 50% ownership
- Exempt property allowance
- Family allowance available

### No State Income Tax on Retirement
- Social Security not taxed
- Favorable treatment of retirement income
- Benefits beneficiaries receiving inherited retirement accounts
- Arizona is tax-friendly for retirees

### Planning Considerations
- Use community property agreement to ensure proper classification
- Consider beneficiary deeds for real property
- Community property trust can maintain character in revocable trust
- Coordinate federal and community property planning for maximum basis step-up
- Prenuptial agreements should address community property

## Safe Deposit Box Rules

### Access After Death
- Joint renter has immediate access
- Without joint renter: court order or letters testamentary required
- Bank may allow inventory-only access with death certificate (no removal)
- Executor/administrator access with letters testamentary

### Joint Ownership
- Surviving joint renter has full access rights
- Joint renter can remove contents without restriction
- No automatic freeze on death of one renter
- Consider: joint rental may expose contents to co-renter's creditors

### Best Practices
- Original will should NOT be stored in safe deposit box (may delay access)
- Keep copy of box inventory with estate planning documents
- Inform executor of box location and access method
- Consider naming executor as joint renter for estate planning purposes

## State Charitable Incentives

### Arizona Charitable Tax Credit
- Credit for donations to Qualifying Charitable Organizations (QCOs)
- Single: up to $470 credit (2025)
- Married filing jointly: up to $938 credit (2025)
- Dollar-for-dollar credit, not just deduction

### Qualifying Foster Care Charitable Organizations (QFCOs)
- Separate credit for donations to QFCOs
- Single: up to $587 credit (2025)
- Married filing jointly: up to $1,173 credit (2025)
- Can claim both QCO and QFCO credits

### School Tax Credits
- Public school extracurricular credit: up to $400 single / $800 joint
- Private school tuition organization credit: up to $1,459 single / $2,609 joint

### Estate Planning Implications
- Lifetime giving during Arizona residency maximizes state tax benefits
- Credits are non-refundable but reduce Arizona income tax dollar-for-dollar
- Coordinate with federal charitable deduction planning
- Can significantly reduce state income tax liability
