# Texas

## Key Differences from Other States
- **Community property state**
- No state estate tax (constitutionally prohibited)
- No state inheritance tax
- **No state income tax** (constitutionally prohibited)
- **Strongest homestead protection** (unlimited value, 10/200 acres)
- Independent administration widely used
- Lady Bird Deed available
- Holographic wills recognized

## Community Property Rules
**Texas Family Code Title 1**

### Classification
- All property acquired during marriage is **community property**
- Separate property: before marriage, gifts, inheritance
- Income from separate property is **community property** (civil law rule)
- Appreciation of separate property may be community
- Full step-up in basis at first spouse's death

### Estate Planning Implications
- Each spouse owns 50% of community property
- Full step-up in basis for **both halves** at first death
- Each spouse can dispose of their 50% by will
- Significant income tax savings from double step-up

### Marital Property Agreements
- Spouses can agree to convert community to separate (and vice versa)
- Must be in writing and signed by both spouses
- Partition and exchange agreements
- Prenuptial and postnuptial agreements available

### Characterization Issues
- Tracing required to prove separate property
- Commingling can convert separate to community
- Keep records of separate property sources

## Will Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Texas Estates Code Title 2

### Execution Requirements (Tex. Est. Code § 251.051)
- Testator must be **18+ years old** (or married, or in military)
- Must be of **sound mind**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by testator**
- Must be **attested by 2 credible witnesses** (14+ years old)
- Witnesses must sign in testator's presence

### Self-Proving Affidavit (Tex. Est. Code § 251.104)
- Available and **strongly recommended**
- Testator and witnesses sign affidavit before notary
- Creates presumption of proper execution
- Avoids need to locate witnesses at probate

### Holographic Wills (Tex. Est. Code § 251.052)
- **Recognized** in Texas
- Must be **entirely in testator's handwriting**
- Must be **signed by testator**
- No witness requirement
- Date recommended but not required

### Witness Requirements
- Must be **credible witnesses** (14+ years old)
- Must sign in testator's presence
- Should not be beneficiaries (best practice)
- Interested witness does not automatically invalidate

## Trust Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: Texas Property Code Chapter 112 (Texas Trust Code)

### Trust Creation (Tex. Prop. Code § 112.001)
- Texas Trust Code governs
- Must have trust property
- Must have ascertainable beneficiaries (or valid purpose)
- Settlor must have capacity
- Writing required for real property

### Trust Administration
- Texas Trust Code governs
- Prudent Investor Rule applies
- Trustee has duties of loyalty, impartiality
- Accounting to beneficiaries required
- Notice upon settlor's death

### Revocable Trusts (Tex. Prop. Code § 112.051)
- Common for probate avoidance
- Settlor retains control during lifetime
- Presumed revocable unless stated otherwise
- Becomes irrevocable at settlor's death

### Directed Trusts (Tex. Prop. Code § 114.0031)
- Texas allows directed trusts
- Trust advisor can direct trustee
- Trustee liability limited when following directions
- Investment direction available

### Trust Decanting (Tex. Prop. Code § 112.072)
- Authorized under Texas law
- Allows modification of irrevocable trusts
- Trustee discretionary power required

## Power of Attorney
**Statutory Basis**: Texas Estates Code Chapter 751 (Durable Power of Attorney Act)

### Execution Requirements (Tex. Est. Code § 751.0021)
- Must be **signed by principal** (or at direction)
- Must be **notarized** (acknowledged)
- Witnesses not required but recommended
- **Statutory form available** and widely used

### Durability (Tex. Est. Code § 751.002)
- Durable if includes durability language
- Non-durable POA terminates upon incapacity
- Springing POA available

### Statutory Form (Tex. Est. Code § 752.051)
- Texas provides **statutory durable POA form** (recommended)
- Other forms valid if properly executed
- Third parties must accept valid POA
- Statutory form widely recognized

### Agent Duties
- Fiduciary duty to principal
- Must act in principal's best interest
- Must keep records
- Must avoid conflicts of interest

### Hot Powers (Tex. Est. Code § 751.031)
- Certain powers require express grant:
  - Create, amend, revoke trusts
  - Make gifts
  - Create/change beneficiary designations
  - Create/change rights of survivorship

### Third Party Acceptance (Tex. Est. Code § 751.201)
- Third parties must accept valid POA
- May request certification
- Refusal may result in liability
- Statutory form enhances acceptance

## Healthcare Directive
**Statutory Basis**: Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 166

### Types Available
- Medical Power of Attorney (Tex. H&SC § 166.152)
- Directive to Physicians (Living Will) (Tex. H&SC § 166.032)
- Out-of-Hospital DNR Order

### Execution Requirements
**Medical POA (Tex. H&SC § 166.152)**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by principal**
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults** (or notarized)
- Witnesses cannot be:
  - Designated agent
  - Healthcare provider
  - Employee of healthcare facility

**Directive to Physicians (Tex. H&SC § 166.032)**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by declarant**
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults**
- Witnesses cannot be:
  - Attending physician
  - Healthcare facility employee
  - Related to declarant

### Agent Authority
- Make healthcare decisions when principal incapacitated
- Access medical records
- Consent to or refuse treatment
- Arrange care and placement
- Make anatomical gift decisions

### Texas POST (Out-of-Hospital DNR)
- Available in Texas
- Out-of-Hospital Do-Not-Resuscitate Order
- Medical orders for seriously ill patients
- Completed with healthcare provider
- Portable across healthcare settings

### Default Surrogate (Tex. H&SC § 166.039)
- If no healthcare directive:
  - Spouse
  - Adult child (majority if multiple)
  - Parent
  - Nearest living relative

## Estate and Inheritance Tax
- **No state estate tax** (constitutionally prohibited)
- **No state inheritance tax**
- **No state gift tax**
- **No state income tax** (constitutionally prohibited)
- Only federal estate tax applies if estate exceeds federal threshold
- 2025 federal exemption: $13.99 million per person

### Constitutional Prohibition
- Texas Constitution prohibits state income and estate taxes
- Very stable tax environment
- Popular domicile for wealthy individuals

### Planning Implications
- No state death tax planning needed
- Focus on federal estate tax for large estates
- Community property provides significant basis step-up
- Probate avoidance for privacy and efficiency

## Medicaid Planning (Texas Medicaid)

### Look-Back Period
- **60 months** (5 years)

### Asset Limits (2025)
- Individual: $2,000
- Married (community spouse): CSRA up to federal maximum (~$154,140)
- CSRA minimum: approximately $31,584

### Income Limits
- Approximately $2,829/month for individuals
- Qualified Income Trust (Miller Trust) available if over limit

### Exempt Assets
- Primary residence (equity limit ~$713,000)
- One vehicle
- Personal belongings and household goods
- Prepaid irrevocable burial contracts
- Term life insurance
- Whole life with face value under $1,500

### Texas-Specific Programs
- **Texas Medicaid** administered by HHSC
- **STAR+PLUS** managed care program
- **Community Based Alternatives** waiver
- Estate recovery program active

### Spousal Protections
- Community spouse resource allowance (CSRA)
- Minimum monthly maintenance needs allowance (MMMNA)
- Court order for increased CSRA available

### Penalty Divisor
- Approximately $5,500-6,500/month (verify current rate)

## Homestead Protection
**Texas Constitution Article XVI, § 50; Texas Property Code Chapter 41**

### Exemption Amount
- **UNLIMITED value** for homestead
- Urban: Up to **10 acres**
- Rural: Up to **200 acres** (family), **100 acres** (single adult)
- **Among strongest protections in US** (with Florida)

### Constitutional Protection
- Protected by Texas Constitution
- Cannot be forced to sell for most debts
- Survives death for surviving spouse and minor children

### Requirements
- Must be **principal residence**
- Must be owner
- Automatic protection (no filing required)
- Declaration can strengthen protection

### Scope of Protection
- Protected from most judgment creditors
- Does NOT protect against:
  - Purchase money mortgages
  - Property tax liens
  - Home equity loans (strict rules)
  - Mechanic's liens (for improvements)
  - Owelty liens from divorce

### Business Homestead
- Business on homestead also protected
- Same acreage limits apply
- Urban: 10 acres
- Rural: 200/100 acres

## Lady Bird Deed (Enhanced Life Estate)
**Common Law Recognized in Texas**

### Features
- Enhanced life estate deed
- Grantor retains **full control** during lifetime
- Can sell, mortgage, or revoke without beneficiary consent
- Avoids probate at death
- **Does not trigger Medicaid transfer penalty**

### Requirements
- Must be in writing
- Must be properly executed (notarized, recorded)
- Names remainder beneficiaries
- Reserves life estate with full powers

### Advantages
- Avoids probate for real property
- Step-up in basis at death
- Retains Medicaid eligibility
- Grantor controls property during lifetime
- Revocable by grantor alone

### Limitations
- Subject to decedent's debts (may vary)
- Only for Texas real property
- Consider title insurance issues

## Independent Administration
**Texas Estates Code Chapter 401**

### Features
- Texas **strongly favors** independent administration
- Minimal court supervision after appointment
- Will should specifically authorize
- Much simpler than dependent administration

### Advantages
- No court approval for routine actions
- Faster estate administration
- Lower costs
- Personal representative has broad authority

### Requirements
- Must be authorized in will (or by agreement)
- Independent executor named
- Bond typically waived
- Annual accounting not required

### Recommendation
- **Every Texas will should authorize independent administration**
- Name independent executor (not administrator)
- Waive bond requirement

## Probate Procedures

### Independent Administration (Tex. Est. Code Ch. 401)
- Preferred method in Texas
- Minimal court involvement
- Independent executor acts without supervision
- Most common form

### Dependent Administration (Tex. Est. Code Ch. 351)
- Full court supervision
- Required if not authorized in will
- More expensive and time-consuming
- Court approval for most actions

### Small Estate Procedures
**Small Estate Affidavit (Tex. Est. Code § 205.001)**
- For estates under **$75,000** in personal property
- No will or will does not meet requirements
- 30-day waiting period after death
- No real property (except homestead passing to spouse/children)

**Muniment of Title (Tex. Est. Code § 257.001)**
- For estates with valid will but no debts
- Quick method to transfer property
- No administration required
- 4-year deadline from date of death

### Timeline
- Independent administration: 6-12 months typical
- Dependent administration: 12-24 months
- Small estate: 1-3 months
- Creditor claim period: 4 months from notice

### Court Jurisdiction
- **Statutory Probate Courts** or **County Courts** handle estates
- Larger counties have statutory probate courts
- County where decedent resided has jurisdiction

## Safe Deposit Box Rules

### Access After Death
- Joint renter has immediate access
- Without joint renter: letters testamentary from probate court required
- Texas Estates Code allows examination with death certificate to search for will
- Independent executor (common in Texas) has streamlined access

### Joint Ownership
- Surviving joint renter has full access rights
- No automatic freeze upon death
- Contents accessible without probate involvement
- Community property rules apply to contents

### Community Property Note
- Texas is community property state
- Box contents characterized as community or separate property
- Important for estate planning purposes
- Document characterization

### Independent Administration
- Texas commonly uses independent administration
- Simplifies access for independent executor
- Less court supervision required

### Best Practices
- Original will should NOT be stored in safe deposit box
- Document community vs. separate property in box
- Independent administration recommended for easier access
- Inform executor of box location
