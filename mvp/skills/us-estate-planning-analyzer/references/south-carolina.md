# South Carolina

## Key Differences from Other States
- No state estate tax
- No state inheritance tax
- Common law property state
- **Requires 3 witnesses for wills** (unusual - most states require 2)
- No holographic wills
- No state income tax on Social Security
- Popular retirement destination
- Lower Medicaid CSRA than most states

## Will Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: South Carolina Code Title 62 (Probate Code)

### Execution Requirements (S.C. Code § 62-2-502)
- Testator must be **18+ years old**
- Must be of **sound mind**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by testator** (or by another at direction in presence)
- Must be signed by at least **3 competent witnesses** (UNUSUAL)
- Witnesses must sign in testator's presence

### Self-Proving Affidavit (S.C. Code § 62-2-503)
- Available and recommended
- Testator and all 3 witnesses sign affidavit before notary
- Creates presumption of proper execution
- Avoids need to locate witnesses at probate

### Holographic Wills
- **NOT recognized** in South Carolina
- All wills must be formally witnessed by 3 witnesses
- Out-of-state holographic wills may be challenged

### Witness Requirements
- Must have **3 competent witnesses** (unique requirement)
- Must sign in testator's presence
- Should not be beneficiaries (best practice)
- Interested witness does not automatically invalidate

### Critical Note
- **South Carolina is one of few states requiring 3 witnesses**
- Wills drafted with only 2 witnesses may be invalid
- Check all estate documents for proper witness count
- Out-of-state wills with 2 witnesses may need re-execution

## Trust Requirements
**Statutory Basis**: S.C. Code Title 62, Article 7 (South Carolina Trust Code)

### Trust Creation (S.C. Code § 62-7-402)
- South Carolina Trust Code adopted
- Must have trust property
- Must have ascertainable beneficiaries (or valid purpose)
- Settlor must have capacity
- Writing required for real property

### Trust Administration
- SC Trust Code governs
- Prudent Investor Rule applies
- Trustee has duties of loyalty, impartiality
- Accounting to beneficiaries required
- Notice upon settlor's death

### Revocable Trusts (S.C. Code § 62-7-602)
- Common for probate avoidance
- Settlor retains control during lifetime
- Presumed revocable unless stated otherwise
- Becomes irrevocable at settlor's death

### Directed Trusts
- South Carolina allows directed trusts
- Trust advisor can direct trustee
- Trustee liability limited when following directions

### Trust Decanting (S.C. Code § 62-7-816A)
- Authorized under South Carolina law
- Allows modification of irrevocable trusts
- Trustee discretionary power required

## Power of Attorney
**Statutory Basis**: S.C. Code Title 62, Article 8 (Uniform Power of Attorney Act)

### Execution Requirements (S.C. Code § 62-8-105)
- Must be **signed by principal** (or at direction)
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults**
- Must be **notarized** (acknowledged)
- All three requirements mandatory
- Statutory form available

### Durability (S.C. Code § 62-8-104)
- Durable if includes durability language
- Non-durable POA terminates upon incapacity
- Springing POA available

### Statutory Form (S.C. Code § 62-8-301)
- South Carolina provides statutory form (recommended)
- Other forms valid if properly executed
- Third parties must accept valid POA

### Agent Duties
- Fiduciary duty to principal
- Must act in principal's best interest
- Must keep records
- Must avoid conflicts of interest

### Hot Powers (S.C. Code § 62-8-201)
- Certain powers require express grant:
  - Create, amend, revoke trusts
  - Make gifts
  - Create/change beneficiary designations
  - Create/change rights of survivorship

### Third Party Acceptance (S.C. Code § 62-8-120)
- Third parties must accept valid POA
- May request certification
- Refusal may result in liability

## Healthcare Directive
**Statutory Basis**: S.C. Code §§ 44-77 (Death with Dignity Act) and 62-5-504

### Types Available
- Healthcare Power of Attorney (S.C. Code § 62-5-504)
- Declaration of a Desire for Natural Death (Living Will)
- Combined Advance Directive

### Execution Requirements
**Healthcare POA (S.C. Code § 62-5-504)**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by principal**
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults**
- Notarization recommended

**Living Will (S.C. Code § 44-77-50)**
- Must be **in writing**
- Must be **signed by declarant**
- Must be **witnessed by 2 adults** AND **notarized**
- Witnesses cannot be:
  - Beneficiaries under will
  - Directly responsible for care
  - Healthcare provider

### Agent Authority
- Make healthcare decisions when principal incapacitated
- Access medical records
- Consent to or refuse treatment
- Arrange care and placement
- Make anatomical gift decisions

### South Carolina POST
- Available in South Carolina
- Physician Orders for Scope of Treatment
- Medical orders for seriously ill patients
- Completed with healthcare provider
- Portable across healthcare settings

### Default Surrogate (S.C. Code § 44-66-30)
- If no healthcare directive:
  - Guardian
  - Spouse
  - Adult child (majority decides)
  - Parent
  - Adult sibling
  - Grandparent
  - Adult grandchild

## Estate and Inheritance Tax
- **No state estate tax**
- **No state inheritance tax**
- **No state gift tax**
- Only federal estate tax applies if estate exceeds federal threshold
- 2025 federal exemption: $13.99 million per person

### Tax-Friendly Environment
- No state death taxes
- No state income tax on Social Security
- Lower property taxes (with homestead exemption)
- Popular destination for retirees

### Planning Implications
- No state death taxes simplifies planning
- Focus on federal estate tax for large estates
- Probate avoidance for privacy and efficiency
- Lower Medicaid CSRA requires extra attention

## Medicaid Planning (SC Healthy Connections Medicaid)

### Look-Back Period
- **60 months** (5 years)

### Asset Limits (2025)
- Individual: **$2,000**
- Married (community spouse): CSRA **lower than federal max (~$66,480)**
- CSRA minimum: approximately $31,584
- **Lower CSRA is significant planning consideration**

### Income Limits
- Approximately $2,829/month for individuals
- Qualified Income Trust (Miller Trust) available if over limit

### Exempt Assets
- Primary residence (equity limit ~$713,000)
- One vehicle
- Personal belongings and household goods
- Prepaid irrevocable burial contracts
- Term life insurance
- Whole life with face value under $1,500

### South Carolina-Specific Programs
- **SC Healthy Connections Medicaid** (SC DHHS)
- **Community Choices** waiver for home-based services
- **Lower CSRA** than most states (~$66,480 vs. ~$154,140)
- Estate recovery program active

### Lower CSRA Impact
- Community spouse may retain less (~$66,480)
- Federal maximum not applied
- Requires additional spousal planning
- Consider Medicaid-compliant annuities
- Court-ordered CSRA increase may be necessary

### Spousal Protections
- Community spouse resource allowance (CSRA) - lower cap
- Minimum monthly maintenance needs allowance (MMMNA)
- Court order for increased CSRA available
- Spousal refusal strategy may be considered

### Penalty Divisor
- Approximately $6,000-7,000/month (verify current rate)

## Homestead Protection
**S.C. Code § 15-41-30**

### Exemption Amount
- Up to **$66,075** in equity protected (indexed for inflation)
- Adjusted periodically
- Applies to dwelling and land

### Requirements
- Must be **head of household** (or debtor's dependent)
- Must be primary residence
- Must be owner
- Declaration may be required

### Scope of Protection
- Protected from most judgment creditors
- Does NOT protect against:
  - Mortgages and deeds of trust
  - Property tax liens
  - Mechanic's liens
  - Child support/alimony

### Senior Homestead Tax Exemption
- Additional property tax exemption for 65+
- Helps reduce ongoing property tax burden
- Popular retirement benefit

## Transfer on Death Deed
- **Not available** in South Carolina
- Use revocable trusts for probate avoidance
- Joint ownership with right of survivorship available
- Consider trust ownership for real property

## Probate Procedures

### Informal Administration (S.C. Code § 62-3-301)
- Available for uncontested estates
- Minimal court involvement
- Personal representative appointed by probate judge
- Most common form

### Formal Administration (S.C. Code § 62-3-401)
- Required if will contested
- Full court supervision
- More expensive and time-consuming

### Small Estate Procedures (S.C. Code § 62-3-1201)
- **Affidavit procedure** for estates under **$25,000** in personal property
- 30-day waiting period after death
- No real property included
- Court filing not required

### Summary Administration
- For smaller estates
- Simplified court process
- Faster than full administration

### Timeline
- Regular administration: 8-12 months typical
- Small estate: 2-4 months
- Complex estates: 12-24 months
- Creditor claim period: 8 months from publication

### Court Jurisdiction
- **Probate Court** handles estates
- County where decedent resided has jurisdiction

## Unique State Features

### Three Witnesses Required
- **Unusual requirement** - most states require only 2
- Must verify all wills have 3 witnesses
- Out-of-state wills may need review
- Critical compliance issue

### Popular Retirement Destination
- No state estate or inheritance tax
- No income tax on Social Security
- Lower cost of living
- Favorable property taxes for seniors
- Warm climate

### Lower Medicaid CSRA
- ~$66,480 maximum vs. federal ~$154,140
- Significant planning consideration for married couples
- May require additional planning strategies
- Court orders for increased CSRA

### Planning Considerations
- Verify all wills have 3 witnesses
- Lower Medicaid CSRA requires planning

- Moderate homestead protection

## Safe Deposit Box Rules

### Access After Death
- Joint renter has immediate access
- Without joint renter: letters testamentary from probate court required
- Bank may permit examination with death certificate to locate will
- Personal representative appointment needed for full access

### Joint Ownership
- Surviving joint renter retains full access
- No automatic freeze upon death
- Contents accessible without probate involvement
- Popular with retirees relocating to SC

### Best Practices
- Original will should NOT be stored in safe deposit box
- Remember SC requires **3 witnesses** for valid will (verify documents)
- Document box contents with estate planning attorney
- Inform personal representative of box location

## State Charitable Incentives

### Exceptional SC Tax Credit
- Credit for donations to organizations serving people with disabilities
- Up to **100% credit** on donations (very efficient)
- Individual: up to $300 credit
- Married filing jointly: up to $600 credit
- Full credit, not just deduction

### Industry Partnership Fund Tax Credit
- Credit for contributions to workforce development
- Supports education and training programs
- Business-focused incentive

### Conservation Contribution Credit
- Credit for donated conservation easements
- 25% of fair market value
- Supports land preservation
- Valuable for large landholders

### Textile Communities Revitalization Credit
- Credit for rehabilitating former textile facilities
- Economic development focus

### Estate Planning Implications
- Exceptional SC credit offers 100% efficiency up to limits
- No state death taxes means federal planning is primary focus
- Popular retirement destination - plan charitable giving before relocation
- Conservation credits valuable for large land holdings
- Coordinate SC credits with federal charitable deduction
- Lower Medicaid CSRA requires additional planning for married couples
